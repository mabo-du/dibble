# Go/No-Go: Evaluating Persistent Homology's Predictive Signal for Lithic Edge-Morphology Classification

## The Challenge of Micro-Topographic Classification in Lithic Analysis

The classification of lithic artefacts is a cornerstone of archaeological analysis, providing critical insights into past human behavior, technological capabilities, and cultural practices [[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13752-025-00491-x), [19](https://www.academia.edu/1911251/Fifty_years_of_Emirates_Archaeology_D_T_Potts_and_P_Hellyer_eds_2012_)]. For decades, researchers have relied on quantitative methods to move beyond qualitative typologies, seeking to establish more objective and repeatable means of categorizing stone tools [[1](https://mnhn.hal.science/mnhn-03431486/document), [68](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/251573446_A_case_of_techno-typological_lithic_variability_and_continuity_in_the_Late_LowerPalaeolithic)]. Central to this endeavor has been the development of morphometric techniques, which aim to capture the geometric properties of artefacts in a numerical format suitable for statistical and machine learning models [[46](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8582405/), [83](https://www.academia.edu/Documents/in/Traditional_Morphometrics)]. However, despite significant advancements, a persistent challenge remains: the accurate classification of tools based on subtle differences in their edge morphology, particularly those defined by fine-scale surface textures rather than gross shape [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. This difficulty represents a critical gap in analytical capability, as these very distinctions often hold the key to understanding tool function, reduction sequences, and technological organization [[8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352409X26002063), [11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41982-024-00178-y)]. The research goal of evaluating Persistent Homology (PH) is directly motivated by this unresolved problem, focusing on two archetypal classification tasks where current methods are theorized to be weakest.

The first and most salient task is the distinction between denticulates and scrapers. These two tool types are fundamentally differentiated by the pattern of modification along their edges [[59](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/prehistoric-stone-tools-of-eastern-africa/retouched-pieces/6F7B6C36A086F1409C0AD1286409B7B7)]. A scraper is typically defined as an artefact bearing a continuous and regularized retouched edge, designed for working organic materials like hide or wood [[60](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0047248425000697)]. This edge can be abrupt, semiabrupt, or shallow but invasive [[60](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0047248425000697)]. In some cases, they are characterized by a flat platform oriented perpendicularly to a carinated edge, with semi-peripheral, unidirectional wear concentrated on the convex surface [[57](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1631068317301112)]. Conversely, a denticulate is defined as a flake or blade possessing two or more contiguous notches [[88](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/251565327_Application_of_a_new_typological_approach_to_classifying_denticulate_and_notched_tools_The_study_of_two_Mousterian_lithic_assemblages)]. These notches, or denticles, are created through a series of small, controlled percussion blows that remove material from the edge, creating a serrated profile [[14](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/233634991_Experimentation_in_the_Formation_of_Edge_Damage_A_New_Approach_to_Lithic_Analysis)]. The distinction between these forms hinges entirely on the micro-topography of the edge—the precise spacing, depth, curvature, and arrangement of concavities and ridges [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. While macro-morphological features such as overall length, width, and angles may provide some general information about the blank, they are inherently incapable of capturing the nuanced textural signature that defines a scraper versus a denticulate [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. The correct identification of these tool classes is archaeologically significant; for instance, the emergence of heavy-duty scrapers is often interpreted as a signal of changing subsistence economies, indicating a greater emphasis on woodworking or hide processing [[57](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1631068317301112)].

The second challenging classification task is the differentiation between retouched and unmodified flakes. Unretouched flakes constitute the overwhelming majority of most lithic assemblages and represent the primary product of core reduction [[12](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0305440314001319), [71](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/47383294_Frequency_and_distribution_of_edge_damage_on_Middle_Stone_Age_lithic_points_Pinnacle_Point_13B_South_Africa)]. They are formed by the fracture of a stone core and possess sharp edges resulting from the propagation of the conchoidal fracture. Retouch, by definition, is the deliberate modification of a flake's edge or surface through the removal of small flakes to create or maintain a functional edge [[14](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/233634991_Experimentation_in_the_Formation_of_Edge_Damage_A_New_Approach_to_Lithic_Analysis)]. This process is a proxy for tool use and indicates a level of investment in shaping the artefact for a specific purpose [[12](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0305440314001319)]. The distinction between a naturally fractured edge and one that has been intentionally retouched lies in the characteristics of the flake scars themselves: their density, orientation, size, and spatial patterning [[11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41982-024-00178-y), [69](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/262679418_An_experimental_assessment_of_the_influences_on_edge_damage_to_lithic_artifacts_A_consideration_of_edge_angle_substrate_grain_size_raw_material_properties_and_exposed_face)]. An unmodified flake will display a random distribution of scars reflecting the original fracture event, whereas a retouched edge will exhibit a more organized, linear sequence of small, invasive scars [[60](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0047248425000697)]. Some flakes may also show edge gloss from use without being formally retouched, further complicating the classification [[40](https://s3.us-west-1.wasabisys.com/p-library/books/e7731520deac134583090dbb6c93119c.pdf)]. The ability to accurately classify these two categories is fundamental to reconstructing the entire reduction sequence, from initial core preparation to final tool use and discard [[29](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41982-022-00115-x), [110](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41982-024-00205-y)]. It allows researchers to distinguish between blanks destined for further modification, tools used for cutting, and items discarded immediately after production [[93](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/327864733_On_the_Function_of_Late_Acheulean_Stone_Tools_New_Data_From_Three_Specific_Archaeological_Contexts_at_the_Lower_Palaeolithic_Site_of_Revadim_Israel)]. Ignoring unretouched flakes leads to a biased understanding of past technologies, as they form the bulk of the evidence [[12](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0305440314001319)].

These two classification problems—denticulate vs. scraper and retouched vs. unmodified flake—are representative of a broader class of challenges in lithic analysis that revolve around edge-morphology. They are distinguished not by the overall shape or size of the artefact, but by the fine-scale, textural properties of its margins. Traditional morphometric approaches, while powerful for many applications, are built upon principles that struggle to capture this type of variation. These methods typically rely on measurements of linear dimensions, angles, and areas, or on the coordinates of discrete landmarks placed on an object [[80](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Traditional-and-Modern-Morphometrics%3A-Review-ma/494f4ad6ae2809b2d874aef8e6a8c97940b2adfe), [85](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10757314/)]. While modern geometric morphometrics (GM) offers a more sophisticated approach by analyzing the configuration of landmarks, it still primarily captures global shape information and requires careful, often PCA-based, alignment of specimens to a common coordinate system [[47](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-1729/12/7/957), [95](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/380621732_A_practical_step-by-step_guide_to_taxonomic_comparisons_using_Procrustes_geometric_morphometrics_and_user-friendly_software_part_A_introduction_and_preliminary_analyses)]. This alignment process itself introduces a sensitivity to orientation that can obscure subtle, local variations in surface texture [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. Furthermore, standard GM does not inherently account for the complex three-dimensional geometry of surfaces, focusing instead on planar configurations or requiring complex adaptations to work with 3D data [[79](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9315565/), [81](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/334201746_Geometric_vs_traditional_morphometric_methods_for_exploring_morphological_variation_of_tadpoles_at_early_developmental_stages)]. Consequently, there exists a clear need for a feature engineering method capable of quantifying the micro-topography of lithic edges—the very "texture" of the surface—as this holds the key to resolving these difficult classification tasks.

The literature in related fields provides strong motivation for exploring new analytical techniques. The study of microtopography is central to other domains of scientific inquiry. For example, in biology and medicine, confocal profilometry is used to reconstruct and measure the microtopography of surfaces to extract tribological features, which is essential for understanding biological processes [[34](https://www.mdpi.com/2571-9408/6/6/252)]. Similarly, in dentistry, artificial intelligence is being applied to improve diagnostic accuracy by analyzing the microtopography of teeth [[22](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jcpe.14160)]. In materials science, the analysis of surface texture is crucial for assessing properties and wear [[7](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11362603/)]. These fields have successfully employed advanced imaging and analysis techniques to move beyond simple shape metrics and quantify surface texture. Archaeology is beginning to leverage similar technologies, such as 3D microscopy and high-resolution photogrammetry, to capture the microtopography of artefacts [[6](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225469871_Visual_Analysis_in_Archaeology_An_Artificial_Intelligence_Approach), [23](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3263/7/4/96)]. The challenge has been to develop robust computational methods that can translate these rich 3D datasets into meaningful, predictive features. The application of Topological Data Analysis (TDA), and specifically Persistent Homology, represents a direct attempt to bridge this gap. TDA offers a mathematical framework for characterizing the shape and structure of data, regardless of its dimensionality, making it uniquely suited to analyze the complex, multi-scale nature of surface textures found on lithic artefacts [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. By framing the problem in terms of topological invariants, PH aims to provide a descriptor that is both sensitive to fine-scale details and invariant to certain transformations like rotation, thereby addressing the core limitations of existing morphometric approaches [[107](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8462731/), [109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. The success of this research would therefore depend on whether PH can effectively translate the visual and tactile perception of edge texture into a set of numerical features that a classifier can learn from, ultimately leading to improved performance on these challenging, archaeologically significant tasks.

## Baseline Morphometric Features and Their Limitations

The baseline model under evaluation relies on a set of 32 existing morphometric features. Although the specific composition of this feature set is not detailed in the provided context, its nature can be inferred from established practices in archaeological and biological sciences. These features likely represent a combination of "traditional morphometrics" and potentially some elements of "geometric morphometrics" (GM). Traditional morphometrics involves the measurement of simple linear distances (length, width, height), areas, angles, and derived ratios [[83](https://www.academia.edu/Documents/in/Traditional_Morphometrics), [85](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10757314/)]. This approach has been foundational in quantitative studies, including age estimation from mandibles [[85](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10757314/)] and the analysis of nuclear morphometrics for medical diagnosis [[46](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8582405/), [84](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6694/13/21/5463)]. Geometric morphometrics represents a more advanced methodology that seeks to preserve more comprehensive shape information by digitizing the positions of anatomical landmarks and analyzing their configuration [[80](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Traditional-and-Modern-Morphometrics%3A-Review-ma/494f4ad6ae2809b2d874aef8e6a8c97940b2adfe)]. Studies comparing traditional and geometric morphometrics consistently find that GM is superior because it protects more morphological information and permits more powerful statistical analyses [[80](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Traditional-and-Modern-Morphometrics%3A-Review-ma/494f4ad6ae2809b2d874aef8e6a8c97940b2adfe), [81](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/334201746_Geometric_vs_traditional_morphometric_methods_for_exploring_morphological_variation_of_tadpoles_at_early_developmental_stages)]. For instance, GM has been successfully applied to analyze tadpole shapes [[81](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/334201746_Geometric_vs_traditional_morphometric_methods_for_exploring_morphological_variation_of_tadpoles_at_early_developmental_stages)] and to compare different morphometric pipelines for accuracy [[82](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2509.00650)]. In archaeology, GM is used to analyze flake shape [[67](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/222529963_A_crossbeam_co-ordinate_caliper_for_the_morphometric_analysis_of_lithic_nuclei_A_description_test_and_empirical_examples_of_application)] and to explore techno-typological variability [[68](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/251573446_A_case_of_techno-typological_lithic_variability_and_continuity_in_the_Late_LowerPalaeolithic)].

Despite their utility and widespread adoption, these traditional and geometric morphometric features share a fundamental limitation when applied to the specific problem of classifying artefacts based on subtle edge-morphology: they are predominantly focused on gross shape and fail to adequately capture fine-scale surface texture. The features are designed to describe the overall form of an object, but they are blind to the intricate patterns of notches, the density of flake scars, or the specific arrangement of ridges and concavities that define denticulates, scrapers, and retouched edges [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. For example, a scraper and a denticulate could easily have identical measurements for length, width, thickness, and angles, yet be completely distinct based on their edge texture. Similarly, an unmodified flake and a lightly retouched flake might share nearly all their traditional morphometric values, as the modifications are confined to a narrow region along the perimeter and do not significantly alter the overall dimensions or mass of the piece [[11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41982-024-00178-y)]. This limitation is not unique to archaeology; even in closely related fields like biology, the transition from simple measurements to more sophisticated surface analysis was necessary to capture functional and pathological states [[34](https://www.mdpi.com/2571-9408/6/6/252)].

Furthermore, many morphometric analyses, particularly those involving geometric morphometrics, require a preprocessing step of Procrustes superimposition or Principal Component Analysis (PCA) alignment. This process aligns all specimens to a common coordinate system, typically by centering them and rotating them to minimize variance [[95](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/380621732_A_practical_step-by-step_guide_to_taxonomic_comparisons_using_Procrustes_geometric_morphometrics_and_user-friendly_software_part_A_introduction_and_preliminary_analyses)]. While this is effective for comparing gross shapes, it introduces a dependency on the initial orientation of the artefact. A slight difference in how an artefact is placed on the scanner or how a researcher places landmarks can lead to different alignments and, consequently, different feature values. This sensitivity to orientation is a known weakness and a potential source of error [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. In contrast, Persistent Homology is inherently rotation-invariant; its output descriptors are properties of the intrinsic shape of the data cloud and remain unchanged regardless of how the object is rotated in space [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. This property alone makes it theoretically better suited for analysing artefacts where consistent orientation during 3D scanning or landmark placement might be difficult to achieve.

The reliance on these features also implies a specific conceptualization of shape. Traditional morphometrics reduces shape to a set of scalar values, while even GM, though more holistic, ultimately analyzes the Cartesian coordinates of discrete points. Neither paradigm is inherently equipped to describe the "loops" and "voids" that characterize a textured surface. They are excellent at describing smooth, convex objects but struggle with the concavities, irregularities, and discontinuities that are the hallmark of lithic surfaces. The existing 32-feature model likely represents a well-calibrated and validated system for general lithic classification, capable of achieving high accuracy in many contexts [[47](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-1729/12/7/957), [49](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Classification-accuracy-compared-with-baseline-models-Shown-are-area-under-the-receiver_fig1_367328869)]. However, its performance is expected to reach a ceiling when faced with tasks that demand an understanding of micro-topography. The hypothesis driving this research is that this ceiling exists precisely because the features are orthogonal to the information contained in the fine-scale surface texture. In other words, the 32 features already capture everything they can about the gross shape, and any remaining discriminatory power lies in the domain of texture, which they cannot access. Therefore, adding a new class of features that specifically targets this domain is hypothesized to push the classifier's performance beyond its current limits.

This limitation is not merely theoretical; it reflects a long-standing practical challenge in the field. For centuries, the identification of tool function based on edge attributes relied on manual observation under a microscope [[4](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/283835285_Surface_analysis_of_stone_and_bone_tools)]. Microwear analysis, which examines microscopic damage and polish on tool edges, remains a highly specialized and time-consuming discipline [[65](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/229377650_Blind_tests_shed_light_on_possibilities_and_limitations_for_identifying_stone_tool_prehension_and_hafting)]. While automated methods for microwear analysis are emerging, integrating this type of fine-scale data into large-scale, quantitative classification frameworks remains a significant hurdle [[34](https://www.mdpi.com/2571-9408/6/6/252), [103](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Ellen-Schulz-Kornas)]. Machine learning models trained on traditional morphometric features are thus operating with incomplete information for these specific tasks. They are like classifiers trying to distinguish between different breeds of dogs based only on their weight and height, while the actual distinguishing features lie in ear shape, fur texture, and tail carriage. The goal of introducing PH features is to provide the model with information about the "fur texture" and "ear shape"—the micro-topographic details—that are currently missing from its input. The success of the project hinges on whether this new information is genuinely predictive and whether the classifier can effectively learn to use it to make more accurate distinctions between denticulates and scrapers, and between retouched and unmodified flakes.

## Persistent Homology as a Novel Feature Engineering Approach

Persistent Homology (PH) emerges as a compelling solution to the identified limitations of traditional morphometric features by offering a mathematically rigorous way to quantify the shape and texture of complex datasets, such as 3D point clouds representing lithic artefacts. As a core component of Topological Data Analysis (TDA), PH is designed to extract robust, multi-scale features that describe the underlying structure of data, independent of its embedding in space [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. Its strength lies in its ability to track the evolution of topological features—such as connected components, loops (1-dimensional holes), and voids (2-dimensional holes)—as a scale parameter, known as the filtration parameter, is gradually increased [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. This process produces a "persistence diagram," a graphical summary that records the "birth" (creation) and "death" (cancellation) of each topological feature across scales. The persistence diagram serves as a compact, rotation-invariant descriptor of the object's shape, capturing information at multiple levels of detail simultaneously [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)].

The theoretical promise of PH for the specified classification tasks stems directly from its capacity to capture micro-topography, which is precisely what traditional morphometrics misses. For the distinction between denticulates and scrapers, the key differentiating factor is the presence of notches or concavities along the edge. In the language of topology, these concavities manifest as 1-dimensional loops or holes. As the filtration progresses, small, isolated pockets of points corresponding to these notches will form connected components (a birth event), which then merge with the main body of the artefact as the scale increases, forming a loop (another birth event). This loop will persist until the filtration scale becomes large enough to "fill in" the notch, at which point the loop will disappear (a death event). The resulting persistence bar in the 1-dimensional diagram, connecting the birth and death times, encodes the significance of that particular notch—in terms of its depth and longevity across scales. A deep, well-defined notch characteristic of a denticulate will produce a long persistence bar, while a shallow or poorly defined concavity might produce a short one. This directly translates the visual perception of a "notch" into a measurable, numerical feature.

Similarly, for the classification of retouched vs. unmodified flakes, PH is ideally suited to quantify the density and texture of flake scars. Flake scars are essentially small depressions and ridges on the surface of the artefact. While individual scars may be too small to form a complete loop, the collective pattern of scars creates a textured surface. Short-persistence features, which are often discarded as noise in other contexts, become highly informative here. These short bars in the persistence diagrams correspond to very small-scale topological features that exist for only a brief period in the filtration. They encode information about the local surface roughness and scar density [[108](https://arxiv.org/html/2307.09259v2), [109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. A heavily retouched edge, covered in a dense array of small scars, would generate a persistence diagram rich with short-persistence bars in the 1-dimensional diagram, reflecting the multitude of tiny concavities. An unmodified flake, with fewer and larger scars from the initial break, would have a different distribution of persistence bars, likely with fewer short bars and potentially longer ones corresponding to the larger original scars. Thus, PH provides a mechanism to numerically summarize the "texture" of the edge, a quality that is intuitive to a human expert but extremely difficult to quantify with traditional metrics.

A significant advantage of PH, as noted in the proposal, is its inherent rotation and translation invariance [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. Because the persistence diagram describes the internal relationships between points in the cloud, its value does not change if the entire artefact is rotated or translated in 3D space. This contrasts sharply with morphometric features that may require PCA alignment, a process that can introduce errors and biases, especially for objects with complex shapes [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. This property simplifies the data preprocessing pipeline considerably, as there is no need for a separate, potentially error-prone alignment step before feature extraction. This robustness to orientation is a major practical benefit for archaeological applications, where artefacts may be scanned from various angles or orientations.

The implementation plan proposes a pragmatic and computationally tractable workflow. It begins with the raw 3D data, which for each artefact consists of a vertex cloud of approximately 5,000 points. Sub-sampling the full-resolution mesh to this size is a critical step that balances the need for sufficient detail to capture micro-topography with the computational constraints of calculating persistent homology, which can be intensive for very large point sets [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. The next step is to compute the persistence diagrams using an Alpha complex filtration. An Alpha complex is a simplicial complex derived from the point cloud that approximates the shape of the underlying surface. It is a popular choice for PH computation due to its efficiency and effectiveness in many applications [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. The result is a set of persistence diagrams for dimensions 0, 1, and 2, representing changes in connected components, loops, and voids, respectively. For the classification tasks at hand, the 0-dimensional diagram (capturing the number of pieces) and the 1-dimensional diagram (capturing notches and scars) are expected to be the most informative, while the 2-dimensional diagram (capturing enclosed volumes or cavities) may provide supplementary information.

Finally, these persistence diagrams, which are multisets of points in the plane, must be converted into a fixed-length numerical vector so they can be used as features in a machine learning model. The proposal specifies the use of "persistence images" as the vectorization method [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. Persistence images are a popular and effective technique that involves transforming the persistence diagram into a density-like image. This is achieved by representing each point in the diagram as a blurred version of a delta function, weighted by its persistence, and then summing these blurred functions over all points in the diagram. The resulting image can then be flattened into a vector of pixel intensities. This process converts the abstract, set-based representation of the persistence diagram into a concrete, numerical format that is easy for classifiers to process. The resolution and parameters of the image (e.g., the kernel function, bandwidth, and grid size) act as tuning parameters for the feature extraction pipeline itself. While the initial signal test uses a fixed setup, a full implementation would involve optimizing these parameters to maximize performance. The output of this process is a set of tens or hundreds of new topological features per artefact, which are then concatenated with the original 32 morphometric features to form the enhanced feature set for the classifier [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)].

While the theoretical foundations of PH are strong, its application is not without challenges. The sensitivity of PH to noise is a critical consideration [[107](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8462731/)]. Lithic surfaces are rarely pristine; they are subject to weathering, post-depositional damage, and manufacturing variability, all of which contribute to noise in the 3D scan data. The choice of filtration method and vectorization technique can influence the sensitivity to this noise [[107](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8462731/)]. However, PH is generally considered to be robust to moderate levels of noise, as small perturbations in the data will typically only affect short-persistence features, which represent local, fine-scale structures [[107](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8462731/)]. The features derived from short-persistence bars, which are theorized to capture texture, might also be highly sensitive to noise. The success of the signal test will depend on whether the true signal from the tool-making process is strong enough to be distinguishable from this background noise. The feasibility of applying PH to archaeological data is supported by analogous studies, such as the use of 3D surface texture analysis for taphonomic assessment of bone tools [[103](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Ellen-Schulz-Kornas)], and the reconstruction of microtopography with confocal profilometry for extracting tribological features [[34](https://www.mdpi.com/2571-9408/6/6/252)]. These examples demonstrate that quantifying fine-scale surface texture is a viable analytical goal, and PH provides a powerful, albeit complex, toolkit to achieve it.

## Pragmatic Implementation Strategy and Risk Mitigation

The research project is structured around a highly pragmatic and efficient decision-making framework designed to deliver a clear "go/no-go" verdict on the utility of Persistent Homology within a constrained timeframe. This strategy prioritizes speed and risk mitigation, acknowledging that the full optimization of a PH-based feature extraction pipeline is a substantial undertaking that could take up to ten days [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. Instead of embarking on this full-scale effort immediately, the plan initiates with a minimal signal test—a rapid pilot study intended to validate the fundamental premise before committing significant resources. This approach is characteristic of agile development methodologies applied to scientific research, allowing for iterative validation and preventing wasted effort on unpromising avenues. The core of this strategy is a focused investigation on two of the most challenging binary classification pairs, using a streamlined implementation of PH with fixed parameters to isolate the question of whether any predictive signal exists at all.

The minimal signal test is designed to be completed in approximately two days and follows a straightforward procedure. First, a small, balanced subset of 100 artefacts is selected, comprising 50 examples from one class and 50 from another (e.g., 50 denticulates and 50 scrapers) [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. This small sample size is a critical element of the rapid testing strategy, as it drastically reduces the computational load compared to processing all 3,415 artefacts. Second, PH features are computed for these 100 artefacts using a pre-determined, simplified pipeline. As specified, this involves creating a sub-sampled vertex cloud of approximately 5,000 points for each artefact, computing the persistence diagrams via an Alpha complex filtration, and vectorizing these diagrams into a feature vector using persistence images [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. By fixing these parameters—filtration type, vectorization method, and sub-sample size—the effort is concentrated on the central question of signal detection rather than on the complex task of hyperparameter tuning, which is reserved for a potential full implementation phase. Third, a simple 2-class classifier is trained on the combined feature set (the original 32 morphometric features plus the newly generated PH features). Finally, the performance of this augmented model is evaluated using cross-validation. The critical decision point is whether the cross-validated accuracy exceeds the accuracy of the baseline model (using only the 32 features) by at least 1 percentage point [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. If the threshold is met, it constitutes a positive signal, justifying the investment in a full-scale implementation. If not, the conclusion is that this specific approach to PH does not yield a discernible predictive advantage for the given tasks, and the project should proceed without it.

This minimalist approach is complemented by a proactive assessment of potential risks and their corresponding mitigation strategies, demonstrating a mature understanding of the technical and logistical hurdles involved. One of the primary risks is that PH might add no predictive value whatsoever [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. This is the central hypothesis of the signal test. The mitigation for this risk is the signal test itself; by aborting the process if no significant signal is found in the initial 100 artefacts, the project avoids proceeding with a method that is unlikely to succeed [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. Another significant risk is computational cost. Calculating persistent homology, especially on 3D point clouds, can be computationally demanding. The plan mitigates this by selecting `gudhi`, a library with a fast C++ backend, which is estimated to take only about two seconds per mesh for a 5,000-point Vietoris-Rips filtration [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. As a fallback, the pure Python `ripser` library is available, ensuring that the pipeline can be run even on less powerful hardware, albeit potentially slower [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)].

A third risk relates to the quality of the input data. Many PH algorithms are designed to work with meshes, and non-watertight or poorly constructed meshes can cause the computation to fail [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. To mitigate this, the proposed strategy shifts from mesh filtration to working directly with the vertex cloud. Since the PH computation is performed on the point cloud data itself (e.g., via Alpha or Vietoris-Rips complexes), the topological structure of the underlying surface is captured without relying on the explicit triangulation of the mesh [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. This makes the pipeline more robust to imperfections in the 3D reconstruction process, a common issue when dealing with real-world artefacts. The risk of feature explosion leading to overfitting is also acknowledged. Adding dozens of new features could increase model complexity and harm generalization, especially if the signal is weak [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. The mitigation for this is the inclusion of a dimensionality reduction step (e.g., PCA or UMAP) *before* concatenating the PH features with the original 32. This compresses the new features into a smaller number of principal components (e.g., 5-15), reducing the total feature count and helping to prevent the model from memorizing noise in the training data [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)].

Finally, software dependencies and installation issues present a minor risk. The successful execution of the pipeline depends on libraries like `gudhi` and `persim`. While these are standard packages, installation can sometimes encounter platform-specific issues [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. The mitigation strategy is straightforward: the code is designed to fall back to `ripser` if `gudhi` is unavailable, ensuring that the core logic remains functional across different environments [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. The following table summarizes the proposed implementation strategy and its associated risk mitigation measures.

This comprehensive and pragmatic approach transforms an open-ended research question into a series of discrete, manageable, and testable hypotheses. It acknowledges the potential of PH while grounding its evaluation in empirical evidence gathered efficiently. The decision to deprioritize feature interpretability in favor of pure predictive performance gain aligns perfectly with this pragmatic framework. The ultimate goal is not necessarily to understand *why* a PH feature improves accuracy, but simply to confirm that it does. If the signal test passes, the focus can shift to optimizing the pipeline for maximum performance. If it fails, the project can pivot away from PH without having incurred the costs of a full, fruitless implementation. This structured, risk-aware methodology is the most critical element for achieving the user's goal of a swift and decisive evaluation.

## Expected Outcomes and Strategic Implications

The outcome of the proposed minimal signal test will have profound strategic implications for the direction of the research and the broader application of advanced computational methods in lithic analysis. The evaluation is framed as a binary decision point: either Persistent Homology provides a sufficient signal to improve classification accuracy by at least 1 percentage point, or it does not. Each possible outcome dictates a distinct path forward, with significant consequences for resource allocation, methodological development, and the potential advancement of archaeological science. The analysis of these outcomes moves beyond mere technical results to consider their impact on the research trajectory and the validity of the underlying hypothesis.

If the signal test yields a success, meaning the classifier incorporating PH features achieves a cross-validated accuracy that is at least 1 percentage point higher than the 32-feature baseline, the immediate implication is that PH has demonstrated tangible value. This positive result would serve as a powerful proof-of-concept, validating the core hypothesis that topological features capturing micro-topography contain predictive information not present in traditional morphometric measurements. Such a finding would justify the "go" decision and authorize the progression to a full pipeline integration. This subsequent phase would involve running the now-validated PH feature extraction process on the entire dataset of 3,415 artefacts. More importantly, it would trigger a dedicated optimization effort. With the fundamental premise confirmed, the focus would shift from "if" PH works to "how well" it can work. This would entail a systematic search for optimal parameters within the PH pipeline. This includes experimenting with different filtration methods (e.g., comparing Alpha complex with Vietoris-Rips), varying the sub-sampling density of the point clouds, and tuning the parameters of the persistence image vectorization (e.g., kernel type, bandwidth, image resolution). The goal of this optimization phase would be to maximize the accuracy gain, moving beyond the optimistic but unconfirmed estimates of +1.5 to +3 percentage points [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Example-Persistent-Homology-for-a-point-cloud-and-b-grey-scale-image-objects_fig5_378595008)]. A successful full implementation would represent a significant methodological advance. It would provide archaeologists with a new, powerful tool for automating the analysis of edge morphology, a domain that has historically been reliant on subjective, manual inspection [[4](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/283835285_Surface_analysis_of_stone_and_bone_tools)]. The ability to reliably distinguish between denticulates and scrapers, or retouched and unmodified flakes, based on quantitative, rotation-invariant features could unlock new possibilities for large-scale techno-typological studies, enhance our understanding of reduction sequences, and provide more robust data for interpreting ancient human behavior.

Conversely, if the signal test results in a failure—if the accuracy gain is less than 1 percentage point—the strategic implication is equally clear: the specific implementation of PH tested here does not provide a useful signal for the target classification tasks. This "no-go" decision would lead to the abandonment of PH for this particular purpose. The project would pivot away from this avenue, and resources currently allocated to its development would be reallocated to explore alternative feature engineering approaches or different machine learning architectures. A negative result, while seemingly disappointing, is scientifically valuable. It would provide crucial evidence documenting the limitations of a promising technique in a specific archaeological context. This prevents the waste of future time and effort by other researchers who might otherwise pursue a similar path. The failure could stem from several causes. It might indicate that, contrary to the initial hypothesis, the micro-topographic signal in the dataset is too weak or too noisy to be captured effectively by the chosen PH pipeline. Alternatively, the failure could be attributed to poor parameter selection in the signal test itself; the chosen filtration method, vectorization technique, or sub-sample size might simply not be appropriate for the data, masking a genuine underlying signal. Regardless of the specific reason, the negative outcome provides important negative knowledge. It suggests that either PH is not the right tool for this specific problem, or that a much more sophisticated and time-consuming optimization of the PH pipeline is required to succeed. This would inform future research, highlighting the need for more extensive parameter tuning or perhaps the exploration of more advanced TDA techniques beyond the scope of the initial signal test.

In either case, the strategic implications extend beyond the immediate project. Success would position the research group at the forefront of applying cutting-edge computational methods to archaeological problems, potentially leading to publications, collaborations, and new research directions centered on TDA and machine learning in archaeology. It would lend empirical support to the growing trend of integrating AI and data science into the humanities, demonstrating a clear and measurable impact on a core analytical task. Failure, while a setback for this specific goal, would contribute to a more nuanced understanding of the applicability of TDA. It would underscore the importance of empirical validation before large-scale adoption and highlight the context-dependent nature of algorithmic success. The project's design, with its emphasis on a rapid, empirically-grounded decision, ensures that the research remains agile and responsive. It avoids becoming mired in the complexities of a full implementation without first establishing a solid foundation of evidence. Ultimately, the evaluation of PH is not just about improving a classifier; it is a test of a new paradigm for extracting information from archaeological data. The outcome of this test will determine whether this paradigm offers a viable pathway forward or represents a promising but impractical idea for the specific challenges of lithic classification. The clarity and decisiveness of the proposed go/no-go framework are paramount to navigating this critical juncture in the research.
