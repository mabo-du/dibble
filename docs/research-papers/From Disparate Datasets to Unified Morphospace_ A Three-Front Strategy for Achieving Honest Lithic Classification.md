# From Disparate Datasets to Unified Morphospace: A Three-Front Strategy for Achieving Honest Lithic Classification

This report presents a comprehensive and integrated roadmap designed to address the critical challenge of cross-dataset generalization in a lithic typology classifier. The central problem is the model's catastrophic failure outside its primary training domain, the OAP dataset, which comprises 71% of the training samples . This has resulted in a disconnect where a high 86.1% cross-validation accuracy collapses to a mere 6–12% in leave-one-dataset-out (LOGO) evaluations, rendering the model dishonest and practically useless for classifying artefacts from other traditions like the Levantine Acheulean, COADS projectile points, Lombao experimental cores, and Morales retouch experiments . The user's objective is to develop an implementable strategy that enhances honest cross-dataset performance while maintaining OAP-specific accuracy above a minimum threshold of approximately 80%, adhering to strict CPU-compatible, `scikit-learn`-based constraints and preserving categorical outputs for archaeological publication standards . This report systematically explores three interconnected frontiers: (1) rebalancing strategies to counteract dataset dominance; (2) redefining the typology system through unsupervised diagnostics; and (3) expanding feature representation with a lightweight persistence image pipeline from persistent homology. Each section provides concrete, actionable recommendations grounded in the provided research context.

## Foundational Strategy: Mitigating Dataset Overfitting through Data-Centric Rebalancing

The primary driver of the classifier's failure is not a flaw in the model architecture or feature set but a fundamental data-centric issue: the severe imbalance in the training data . The Random Forest model, an ensemble of decision trees, inherently favors patterns present in larger portions of the data [[1](https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/generated/sklearn.ensemble.RandomForestClassifier.html), [110](https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/random-forest-classifier-using-scikit-learn/)]. With 71% of the training samples originating from the OAP dataset, the model has learned the specific morphological characteristics of European Aurignacian stone tools so deeply that it has mistaken them for universal typological definitions. Consequently, when faced with artefacts from other traditions—such as the morphologically distinct handaxes of the Levantine Acheulean or the projectile points of COADS—the model fails completely, as their geometric signatures do not align with its heavily skewed understanding of "Biface" or "Handaxe" . The solution, therefore, must begin with a foundational strategy of rebalancing the training data to force the model to learn more abstract, generalized typological concepts instead of memorizing a single assemblage. This involves moving beyond simple class-level balancing to address the more complex problem of dataset-level imbalance, all while respecting the critical constraint that OAP-specific performance must remain robust.

A direct and intuitive approach to rebalancing is downsampling the dominant OAP dataset. By reducing the number of OAP samples in each training batch, the model is forced to pay attention to the minority datasets, which contain genuinely different morphologies for the same typological labels . For instance, downsampling OAP from 2,010 to 526 samples, matching the size of the next largest dataset (Levantine Acheulean), would create a perfectly balanced dataset composition. However, this aggressive approach carries significant risks. It discards a vast amount of valuable information contained within the large OAP corpus, which could degrade the very OAP-specific accuracy we aim to preserve at or above the 80% threshold . Furthermore, it may lead to a loss of statistical power for learning the nuanced variations within the OAP tradition itself. A less aggressive downsampling ratio might offer a better trade-off. For example, reducing OAP to 40-50% of the total dataset could strike a balance, providing a meaningful boost to cross-dataset generalization without excessively compromising the model's expertise on its primary use case. While straightforward to implement, downsampling is a blunt instrument that sacrifices information. A more sophisticated and preferred alternative is the application of sample weighting.

Inverse frequency weighting offers a powerful mechanism to rebalance the influence of different datasets without discarding any data points. Scikit-learn's `RandomForestClassifier` supports this through its `sample_weight` parameter, which allows for assigning a weight to each individual training sample [[44](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/72909466/class-weight-and-sample-weight-ineffective-for-sklearn-random-forest)]. The user's request to weight by dataset, rather than just by class, is particularly insightful and directly targets the root cause of the problem. This can be achieved by calculating weights inversely proportional to the size of each source dataset. For example, a sample from the OAP dataset (2,010 samples) would receive a lower weight than a sample from the COADS dataset (492 samples). A practical starting point for these weights would be $w_i = N_{total} / N_{dataset}$, where $N_{total}$ is the total number of samples (3,415) and $N_{dataset}$ is the number of samples in the specific dataset of that sample. This ensures that the total weighted contribution of each dataset to the training process is roughly equal. This method is superior to simple class weighting (`class_weight='balanced'`) because it addresses the dataset-level skew, which is the primary confounder. Class weighting only balances misclassification penalties between the eight typological classes, but since these classes are themselves distributed unevenly across datasets, it fails to solve the core problem [[3](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/17688147/how-to-weight-classes-in-a-randomforest-implementation), [4](https://blog.csdn.net/qq_39805362/article/details/115458071)]. The interaction between `sample_weight` and `class_weight` is additive; the model will consider both the specified class imbalance penalty and the explicit sample weights provided during the construction of each decision tree [[44](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/72909466/class-weight-and-sample-weight-ineffective-for-sklearn-random-forest)]. This dual consideration makes the combined approach highly effective for this specific challenge.

Beyond rebalancing, exploring advanced domain adaptation techniques could provide further improvements, though with greater implementation complexity. Domain adaptation aims to build a model that performs well on a target domain (e.g., Levantine Acheulean) using knowledge from a labeled source domain (e.g., OAP) [[8](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content_cvpr_2018/papers/Pinheiro_Unsupervised_Domain_Adaptation_CVPR_2018_paper.pdf)]. Several unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) methods exist, such as adversarial training, where a discriminator network tries to distinguish whether a feature vector comes from the source or target dataset, forcing the main feature extractor to produce domain-invariant representations [[98](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/ICCV2021W/ILDAV/papers/Qiu_Meta_Self-Learning_for_Multi-Source_Domain_Adaptation_A_Benchmark_ICCVW_2021_paper.pdf), [111](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/CVPR2021/papers/Bai_Unsupervised_Multi-Source_Domain_Adaptation_for_Person_Re-Identification_CVPR_2021_paper.pdf)]. Other methods use Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to minimize the distance between the feature distributions of the source and target domains in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space [[98](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/ICCV2021W/ILDAV/papers/Qiu_Meta_Self-Learning_for_Multi-Source_Domain_Adaptation_A_Benchmark_ICCVW_2021_paper.pdf)]. While powerful, many state-of-the-art UDA libraries are built on deep learning frameworks like PyTorch, which conflicts with the project's CPU-only, `sklearn`-compatible constraint [[5](https://pypi.org/project/skada/), [39](https://pytorch-ada.readthedocs.io/)]. However, simpler, scikit-learn compatible implementations of these ideas may be feasible. For example, a custom pre-processing step could be added to project the feature vectors into a space that minimizes MMD between datasets before feeding them to the RandomForest classifier. Another pragmatic approach is multi-dataset training with dataset-conditional normalisation, where statistics (mean, standard deviation) are calculated separately for each dataset during feature scaling. This acknowledges that the "normal" scale of measurements may differ between traditions. While more complex, these methods represent a longer-term objective after simpler rebalancing strategies have been implemented.

Finally, establishing a correct evaluation protocol is essential for honest progress assessment. The initial 86.1% cross-validation accuracy was a classic case of optimistic bias caused by data leakage from the dominant OAP dataset. The current `Leave-One-Dataset-Out` (LOGO) cross-validation protocol is the gold standard for evaluating the true generalization capability of the model [[9](https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/cross_validation.html)]. To formalize this, a dedicated function should be created that iterates through each of the five source datasets, using one as the held-out test set and the remaining four as the training set. The final reported performance should be the average accuracy across these five LOGO folds. This protocol must be used exclusively for all future model comparisons. Stratified K-Fold cross-validation, while useful for handling class imbalance within a single dataset, is insufficient here because it stratifies on class labels, which are themselves confounded by dataset origin [[40](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/32615429/k-fold-stratified-cross-validation-with-imbalanced-classes), [41](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9967638/)]. A stratified split might, by chance, place all samples of a certain class from a single dataset into the training fold, failing to expose the model's inability to generalize across traditions. Therefore, abandoning the misleading CV score and adopting LOGO-CV as the sole performance metric is a non-negotiable prerequisite for developing a trustworthy classifier.

In summary, the most immediate and effective action is to abandon naive rebalancing and adopt a sophisticated data-centric approach. The recommended first step is to implement dataset-based sampling weights via the `sample_weight` parameter. This method directly confronts the core problem of OAP dominance without sacrificing the valuable information contained within its large sample size, making it the ideal choice for respecting the user's constraint to maintain high OAP-specific accuracy. Downsampling should be considered a secondary option for comparative analysis, while domain adaptation remains a potential future direction once the foundational issues are addressed.

## Diagnostic Refinement: Leveraging Unsupervised Methods to Diagnose Typological Inconsistency

While rebalancing the training data addresses the symptom of dataset dominance, the deeper, more profound bottleneck lies within the typology system itself. The evidence suggests that human experts disagree on classifications for 15-25% of artefacts, and crucially, the same typological labels are often applied to fundamentally different morphologies across different archaeological traditions . For example, an Italian "Side Scraper" and an Ohio "Side Scraper" may occupy entirely different regions of the 32-dimensional feature space. The classifier is being trained on this inconsistent ground truth, forcing it to learn a flawed mapping between geometry and labels. Attempting to improve a model on such a foundation is akin to building on sand. Therefore, a parallel and equally critical frontier is the diagnostic refinement of the typology system. This involves using unsupervised machine learning to move beyond expert-defined labels and discover the natural groupings inherent in the geometric data. The goal is not necessarily to replace the traditional typology immediately but to understand its limitations and identify where it fails to correspond to geometric reality.

Unsupervised clustering is the primary tool for this diagnostic task. By applying clustering algorithms directly to the 32-dimensional morphometric feature space, we can reveal the intrinsic structure of the artefact assemblage. Algorithms like HDBSCAN (Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) are particularly well-suited for this purpose as they do not require specifying the number of clusters beforehand and can identify clusters of varying shapes and densities, as well as noise points that represent outliers or poorly defined artefacts [[29](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666033422000211)]. When the clustering results are visualized (e.g., using UMAP or t-SNE for dimensionality reduction), they can be overlaid with the existing typological labels. This visualization provides an immediate and powerful diagnostic. For instance, a cluster plot might show that all "Handaxe" samples from the Levantine Acheulean form a tight, distinct cluster, while all "Biface" samples from the COADS collection form a completely separate, non-overlapping cluster. Despite both being labeled as "Biface," their geometric realities are worlds apart. This would visually confirm the presence of "inter-source label drift," a key factor in the model's confusion . Conversely, the analysis might reveal that a single traditional label, such as "Scraper," actually corresponds to multiple, geographically or temporally separated clusters, suggesting that it represents more than one underlying morphological concept. Such findings would provide concrete, data-driven evidence for which typological boundaries are arbitrary or problematic.

To complement clustering, creating a continuous morphospace embedding offers another powerful diagnostic lens. Techniques like Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) can reduce the 32-dimensional feature space to a low-dimensional (e.g., 2D or 3D) manifold where the distance between points reflects their morphological similarity [[90](https://joblib.readthedocs.io/en/latest/)]. In this morphospace, artefacts are not confined to discrete boxes but can be placed anywhere in a continuous landscape. This approach is particularly insightful for revealing whether typological boundaries are sharp lines drawn across a smoothly populated space. For example, when plotting the morphospace for blade-like artefacts, one might observe a continuous gradient or "cline" transitioning from "Bladelet" to "Blade." This would suggest that the boundary between these two categories is artificial and that the underlying variation is gradual, challenging the discrete nature of the typology. This aligns perfectly with the user's interest in internal continuous representations as a diagnostic tool . The resulting morphospace can serve as an interactive exploration tool for archaeologists, allowing them to visually inspect the relationships between artefacts and the validity of the existing typological partitions. It can highlight ambiguous cases where an artefact sits precariously on the boundary between two clusters, corresponding to the 15-25% expert disagreement mentioned in the conversation history.

The ultimate goal of this diagnostic phase is not merely to critique the existing typology but to inform a more robust classification workflow. The insights gained from clustering and morphospace analysis can guide several concrete modifications. If the analysis reveals that "Blade" and "Bladelet" are part of a continuous cline, one potential modification is to introduce a hierarchical classification system. Instead of a single classifier trying to distinguish eight disparate types at once, a two-stage cascade could be implemented. The first stage would act as a coarse filter, distinguishing broad morphological groups (e.g., flakes, cores, bifaces). Then, separate, specialized classifiers would be trained for each group to perform fine-grained distinctions (e.g., a Blade/Bladelet classifier for the 'Flake' group, a Handaxe/Picker/Chopper classifier for the 'Biface' group). This approach is inherently more robust because the downstream classifiers operate on much more homogeneous data subsets, avoiding the confusion that arises when trying to classify vastly different morphologies under the same roof [[57](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.18889v1)]. Similarly, if clustering shows that "Side Scraper" from different sites forms distinct clusters, the typology might need to be refined to include tradition-specific variants or to merge them into a more general category like "Scraper."

Another pragmatic approach is to develop tradition-specific label mappings. If the diagnostic analysis confirms that the meaning of "Biface" differs significantly between the Levantine and COADS traditions, the classifier should be made aware of this context. This could be implemented by augmenting the feature vector with a one-hot encoded identifier for the source dataset, effectively teaching the model to expect different morphologies when it sees a COADS sample versus an OAP sample. Alternatively, this could justify training separate models for major traditions, although this moves away from the goal of a single, unified classifier. The most promising path forward, however, is to combine these diagnostics with the proposed hierarchical classifier. The unsupervised analysis can guide the design of the hierarchy—for example, by showing which existing labels naturally group together into broader morphological categories. This reframes the challenge from "improving a bad classifier" to "designing a better classification workflow" that is more aligned with the actual geometric structure of the data.

In conclusion, dedicating effort to diagnosing the typology system is a necessary precursor to building a truly generalizable classifier. By employing unsupervised clustering and continuous morphospace embeddings, we can move from a position of assuming the typology is fixed and correct to one of empirically testing its validity. The output of this diagnostic phase—a clearer understanding of which typological boundaries are arbitrary, which labels suffer from inter-source drift, and which categories are geographically isolated—will provide the blueprint for redesigning the classification task itself, leading to a more accurate and honest final model.

## Advanced Feature Engineering: Implementing a Lightweight Persistence Image Pipeline

The third frontier for improving classifier performance involves expanding the feature set to capture information not represented by the current 32 morphometric features. These features excel at describing local geometry, such as curvature and shape indices derived from vertex neighborhoods, but they may be less adept at capturing global topological properties like connected components, loops, and voids . Persistent homology (PH) is a branch of topological data analysis (TDA) that excels at quantifying these global structural properties in a stable, multi-scale manner [[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.04299v1), [18](https://openreview.net/forum?id=NiCSyYOfex)]. The initial signal test using crude PH summary statistics yielded poor results (41.3% accuracy alone, with no improvement when combined), but this was likely due to an incomplete implementation rather than a lack of signal . A proper implementation using full persistence images—a structured, grid-based representation of the topological information—is expected to yield a more significant signal and provide a novel source of information orthogonal to the existing morphometrics. The challenge is to implement this in a lightweight, fixed-parameter pipeline that meets the runtime constraint of ≤2 seconds per artefact and integrates seamlessly with the existing `sklearn`-compatible feature extraction framework.

To create an implementable and reproducible pipeline, several key parameters must be fixed based on established practices in the TDA literature. First, the input 3D mesh must be normalized to ensure scale invariance. This involves scaling the mesh to fit within a unit bounding box and centering it at the origin [[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.04299v1)]. Second, the choice of filtration is critical. The Vietoris-Rips complex filtration is a robust and computationally efficient choice, as it builds a simplicial complex based on the pairwise distances between points in the point cloud derived from the mesh [[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.04299v1), [118](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.04299)]. While the Alpha complex is more geometrically faithful, the Vietoris-Rips complex is generally more stable and faster to compute, making it suitable for this application. Third, to manage computational cost, especially given the ≤2 second per artefact limit, a subsampling strategy is necessary. Farthest-point sampling is an effective technique that selects a representative subset of vertices from the original mesh, preserving the global structure while drastically reducing computation time [[53](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-4292/15/12/3155)].

Once the persistence diagram (a scatter plot of birth-persistence pairs representing topological features) is computed, it must be converted into a vector format suitable for machine learning. This is where persistence images come in. A persistence image is created by convolving the persistence diagram with a kernel, typically a Gaussian, and then pixelating the resulting surface [[21](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8160457/), [60](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/353522158_A_Comparative_Study_of_Machine_Learning_Methods_for_Persistence_Diagrams)]. The output is a 2D histogram (image) where each pixel's value represents the accumulated "topological density" in that region of the (birth, persistence) plane. Key parameters for this step are the resolution of the image (e.g., 50x50 or 100x100 pixels) and the bandwidth of the Gaussian kernel. The resolution determines the final dimensionality of the feature vector (e.g., a 50x50 image yields a 2500-dimensional feature vector). The kernel bandwidth controls the smoothness of the image; a common heuristic is to set it to half the average persistence value in the diagram [[60](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/353522158_A_Comparative_Study_of_Machine_Learning_Methods_for_Persistence_Diagrams)]. Libraries such as `giotto-tda` provide a robust implementation of this entire pipeline and integrate directly with the `scikit-learn` ecosystem, making it a prime candidate for this project [[72](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2004.02551), [96](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.17340)]. The GUDHI library is another strong option for computing the persistence diagrams [[73](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/71190106/why-do-giotto-tda-and-cripser-give-different-persistence-diagrams-for-cubical-ho)].

The integration of this PH pipeline into the existing workflow requires careful planning to ensure efficiency. Given that PH computation is significantly slower than the current 0.1s per artefact for morphometrics (taking ~2s with GUDHI), a caching strategy is essential for processing new artefacts . The complete feature vector—including the original 32 morphometrics plus the flattened persistence image vector—should be computed once for each artefact in the training set and stored. For inference on a new artefact, the system would first check if the PH features already exist in the cache. If not, it would compute them, add them to the cache, and then use them for prediction. This avoids recomputing expensive features repeatedly. The total model size, consisting of the trained RandomForest and the cached feature values, must remain under 100MB. The persistence image vectors, while high-dimensional, consist of floating-point numbers and should fit within this constraint, especially since only the training set features need to be cached for inference (the model itself is the primary storage).

The expected accuracy impact of adding PH features is a key question. The initial failure of the crude test suggests that the signal is not trivial to extract, but a proper implementation is likely to succeed. The optimistic range of a +1-3 percentage point gain seems plausible. The signal captured by PH is complementary to the local morphology captured by the 32 existing features. For example, handaxes with their characteristic convex lateral edges and bifacial flaking will generate strong, persistent 1-dimensional topological features (loops) that are difficult to describe with local shape indices alone. Cores with complex internal void structures will exhibit strong 2-dimensional features (voids). Blades, with their sharp ridge lines and flake scars, will have a different signature of short-lived 1D features and 0D features (points). This topological signal could help the classifier make more robust distinctions at the highest level (e.g., separating a biface from a core) even when their local geometries are ambiguous. The pessimistic scenario of a +0.5 percentage point gain is also possible if the topological signal is weak or redundant with the existing morphometrics. To decide when to abort the PH experiment, a clear criterion should be established. If, after implementing the full pipeline and combining the PH features with the baseline 32 features, the LOGO-CV accuracy does not show a statistically significant improvement over the best-performing baseline model (from the rebalancing and hierarchical approaches), then further investment in PH should be deprioritized.

By following a standardized, lightweight pipeline and integrating it with a caching strategy, the project can explore this rich source of geometric information without violating its core constraints. The success of this frontier hinges on executing a proper implementation, as the initial crude attempt was likely the source of the poor signal observed previously.

## Hierarchical Classification: A Workflow-Based Approach to Resolving Typological Ambiguity

The most profound limitation of the current classifier is its attempt to solve a definitional problem with a machine learning model. The ambiguity and inconsistency inherent in archaeological typology—where labels like "biface" or "scraper" are applied to geometrically diverse artefacts across different traditions—cannot be fully resolved by simply tweaking a classifier's hyperparameters or adding new features . The solution lies in a fundamental shift in the classification workflow itself. Instead of a single, monolithic classifier tasked with distinguishing eight disparate types simultaneously, the problem should be decomposed into a series of smaller, more manageable sub-problems. The proposed hierarchical cascade classifier is a powerful architectural solution to this challenge, mirroring the way human experts often work: first sorting artefacts into broad, easily distinguishable categories based on gross morphology, and then performing detailed classification within those categories [[57](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.18889v1)]. This approach directly addresses the core issue of typological ambiguity by reducing the scope of each individual classification task, thereby increasing the likelihood of success.

The hierarchical cascade consists of two primary stages. The first stage is a coarse-grained classifier whose sole purpose is to separate the artefacts into major, mutually exclusive morphological groups. Based on the analysis of the source datasets, a logical first-level taxonomy would be: **Flake**, **Core**, and **Biface**. This separation is critical because these three groups represent fundamentally different modes of production and artifact function, and their morphological spaces are largely non-overlapping. The Levantine Acheulean is dominated by handaxes (a Biface type), COADS by projectile points (also Bifaces), OAP by blades and flakes, and the experimental datasets are purely cores and retouched pieces, respectively . By training a dedicated classifier to distinguish these three broad groups, we create cleaner, more homogeneous datasets for the subsequent stages. The features used for this first-stage classifier would be the full 32 morphometrics plus, potentially, the PH features, as distinguishing a core from a biface relies heavily on global shape and volume.

Once the first-stage classifier has assigned an artefact to one of the three groups, it is passed to the corresponding second-stage classifier. Each of these classifiers is specialized and operates on a much narrower morphological space.
*   For the **Flake group**, a classifier would be trained specifically to distinguish between `Blade`, `Bladelet`, `Scrapers`, `Points`, and `Other Flakes`. This is a much easier task than trying to sort all eight types at once.
*   For the **Core group**, a classifier would differentiate between the experimental `Lombao Cores` and the `OAP Cores`. This focuses on the specific reduction strategies present in each tradition.
*   For the **Biface group**, a classifier would be trained to distinguish between `Handaxe` and `Biface` (which would primarily consist of the COADS projectile points). This leverages the fact that while both are bifacial, their plan-view morphologies are distinct enough to be separable.

This cascaded architecture offers several advantages. First, it dramatically reduces the "confusion matrix" for each classifier. A single classifier attempting all eight types must learn to correctly reject seven wrong answers for every right one. In the cascade, the Flake classifier only needs to choose among four types, and it knows with high confidence that it will never see a core or a biface. This specialization leads to higher accuracy within each sub-task. Second, it allows for the development of tradition-specific models at the second level. For instance, the Blade/Bladelet classifier trained on the large OAP dataset is likely to be highly accurate for European Aurignacian material. While this doesn't directly solve the cross-dataset problem, it preserves the high fidelity of the model for its primary use case, which is acceptable if the overall workflow improves the performance on the more challenging, out-of-domain datasets. Third, the architecture is modular and interpretable. If a particular second-stage classifier performs poorly (e.g., the Biface classifier struggles to distinguish Levantine handaxes from COADS points), it can be diagnosed and improved independently without affecting the rest of the system.

From an implementation standpoint within the `scikit-learn` ecosystem, this workflow can be elegantly managed using a custom wrapper class. This wrapper would contain the three first-stage classifiers (though only one is needed for the 'Flake'/'Core'/'Biface' split) and the three corresponding second-stage classifiers. The `predict` method of the wrapper would orchestrate the process: it first calls the first-stage classifier, then uses the result to select the appropriate second-stage classifier, and finally returns its prediction. This maintains the familiar `model.fit()` and `model.predict()` interface expected by users. The training process would involve training each of the four classifiers on their respective subsets of the data. For example, the 'Flake' classifier would be trained on all samples classified as 'Flake' in the training set.

This hierarchical approach also synergizes perfectly with the other interventions in this roadmap. After implementing rebalancing strategies (like dataset-based sampling weights), the data fed into each stage of the cascade will be better balanced. For instance, the data passed to the 'Flake' classifier will contain a more representative mix of Blade, Bladelet, and Scraper samples from all contributing datasets. Similarly, the continuous morphospace embeddings generated from the PH pipeline can be used as an additional diagnostic tool within this framework. By plotting the outputs of the second-stage classifiers on a morphospace, one can visualize whether the model is successfully separating the intended types or if there are still ambiguities that need to be addressed. The hierarchical structure provides a robust framework for managing the inherent complexities of lithic typology, transforming the classification task from a search for a single, all-powerful model into a well-structured, multi-stage workflow that is more resilient, accurate, and aligned with the realities of the data.

## Final Model Integration and Performance Benchmarking

After exploring and implementing the foundational rebalancing strategies, diagnostic typology refinements, and advanced feature engineering, the final phase of this roadmap involves integrating the most successful components into a cohesive final model and subjecting it to rigorous, honest performance benchmarking. The goal is to synthesize the improvements from each frontier into a single, robust classifier that reliably achieves the project's primary objective: prioritized cross-dataset generalization, while maintaining high fidelity for the primary OAP use case. This phase is not about incremental tweaks but about making strategic decisions based on empirical evidence gathered throughout the preceding steps. The final model will likely be a composite of the rebalancing technique (likely dataset-based `sample_weight`), the feature set expansion (likely the addition of persistence image features), and the architectural choice (the hierarchical cascade classifier). The benchmarking will be conducted exclusively using the Leave-One-Dataset-Out (LOGO) cross-validation protocol, which serves as the definitive measure of the model's true utility.

The integration process begins by identifying the best-performing configuration from the previous phases. Let's assume the results indicate that the combination of dataset-based sampling weights, the hierarchical cascade architecture, and the full persistence image features yields the best overall performance. The final model would therefore be an instance of the custom wrapper class described earlier, containing:
1.  A first-stage classifier trained to distinguish 'Flake', 'Core', and 'Biface'. This classifier would be trained using the full training data (all five datasets) but with `sample_weight` applied to balance the influence of each dataset.
2.  Three second-stage classifiers, one for each of the 'Flake', 'Core', and 'Biface' groups. Each of these would also be trained with dataset-balancing weights on their respective subsets of the data. The feature set for all classifiers would be the concatenation of the original 32 morphometrics and the persistence image features (e.g., a 2500-dimensional vector from a 50x50 image).

Once this final model is trained and serialized using a library like `joblib` or `pickle` for efficient persistence, it is ready for the final benchmarking [[22](https://scikit-learn.org/0.18/modules/model_persistence.html), [85](https://scikit-learn.org/stable/model_persistence.html)]. All performance reporting must be based on the LOGO-CV protocol. This means the model's performance will be evaluated five times, each time holding out one of the five source datasets (OAP, Levantine, COADS, Lombao, Morales) and training on the other four. The headline figure for the model's performance will not be a single number but rather a breakdown of its accuracy on each held-out dataset, along with the overall mean. This transparent reporting will demonstrate exactly where the model succeeds and where it struggles, providing a clear picture of its capabilities and limitations.

The addition of a well-implemented persistence image feature set, which adds a novel source of global shape information, could push this ceiling towards **88%**. However, reaching a consistent 90% accuracy across all held-out datasets is likely unattainable given the inherent ambiguity in the typology system itself. Human experts disagree on classifications for 15-25% of artefacts, and a machine learning model cannot resolve this level of fundamental ambiguity . Therefore, the focus must shift from chasing a high percentage to achieving reliable, honest, and interpretable performance. The 90% target should be publicly recalibrated to reflect this more realistic outcome.

## Strategic Synthesis: An Actionable Roadmap for Achieving Honest and Robust Classification

This research has systematically deconstructed the failure of the current lithic typology classifier and constructed a multi-faceted, implementable roadmap to achieve its primary goal: honest and robust cross-dataset generalization. The analysis reveals that the problem is not one of model architecture or feature engineering but is fundamentally data-centric, rooted in extreme dataset imbalance and typological inconsistency. The proposed solution is not a single silver bullet but an integrated strategy that addresses these issues sequentially and synergistically. This final synthesis provides a phased execution plan, establishes realistic performance expectations, identifies critical actions to avoid, and answers the pivotal question of the simplest intervention that delivers the most significant improvement.

The recommended execution roadmap is structured in three distinct phases, ordered by a combination of expected impact and implementation effort.

**Phase 1: Foundational Improvements (High Impact, Low Effort)**
This initial phase focuses on addressing the most immediate and impactful problems with minimal code changes.
1.  **Implement Dataset-Based Sampling Weights:** The very first action should be to modify the RandomForest training loop to utilize scikit-learn's `sample_weight` parameter. Weights should be assigned inversely proportional to the size of each source dataset. This directly counters the OAP dominance that causes the model to overfit, forcing it to pay attention to the morphologically diverse minority datasets. This intervention is expected to yield the most significant and immediate improvement in LOGO-CV accuracy with very low implementation effort. It directly tackles the primary source of dishonesty in the model's performance.
2.  **Refactor to a Hierarchical Cascade Classifier:** Concurrently, the classification workflow should be redesigned from a single flat classifier to a two-stage hierarchical cascade. The first stage would distinguish broad morphological groups ('Flake', 'Core', 'Biface'), and subsequent stages would handle fine-grained classification within these groups. This architectural change is modular and can be implemented with a custom wrapper class around existing `sklearn` estimators. It directly mitigates the confusion caused by trying to classify geometrically disparate artefacts under a single roof.

**Phase 2: Advanced Feature Engineering and Diagnostics (Medium Impact, Medium Effort)**
With the foundational issues addressed, this phase introduces more advanced techniques to refine the model and deepen our understanding of the data.
3.  **Develop and Integrate a Full Persistence Image Pipeline:** Implement the lightweight, fixed-parameter persistence image pipeline as specified earlier. This involves normalizing meshes, computing Vietoris-Rips persistence diagrams, and generating persistence images. Integrate this with the existing feature extractor and caching strategy. The expected accuracy gain is medium, but the value extends beyond performance, providing a novel source of global topological information and a continuous morphospace embedding for diagnostic use.
4.  **Conduct a Comprehensive Typology Refinement Study:** Using the results from the hierarchical model and the new PH-derived morphospace, perform a thorough unsupervised clustering analysis. Visually inspect the clusters against the existing typology to document inconsistencies. Propose concrete changes, such as merging, splitting, or reordering typological classes, based on the empirical evidence of geometric reality.

**Phase 3: Final Evaluation and Iteration (Low Effort, High Value)**
The final phase is dedicated to validating the outcomes and solidifying the new standard for performance reporting.
5.  **Final Performance Benchmarking:** Retrain the final best-performing model configuration using the full suite of improvements (rebalancing, hierarchical structure, PH features) and report its performance exclusively using the honest LOGO-CV protocol. This will provide the definitive, trustworthy measure of the classifier's capabilities.

Based on this integrated roadmap, the realistic new accuracy ceiling for the Basic/Bordes typology is approximately **80-85%**. The ambitious goal of 90% should be recalibrated, as the inherent ambiguity in archaeological typology sets a hard limit on perfect classification . The focus must shift to achieving reliable, cross-dataset performance.

If limited to a single week, the intervention that would deliver the most significant improvement is unequivocally the implementation of **dataset-based sampling weights**. It directly attacks the root cause of the overfitting problem with minimal effort and offers the highest probability of a substantial, immediate lift in honest cross-dataset accuracy.

Finally, it is crucial to know what *not* to do. The following approaches should be explicitly avoided:
*   **Complex, GPU-dependent Domain Adaptation:** Avoid methods reliant on deep learning frameworks like PyTorch or TensorFlow, as they violate the core CPU-only constraint and add unnecessary complexity [[5](https://pypi.org/project/skada/), [39](https://pytorch-ada.readthedocs.io/)].
*   **Forcing Linear Ordinal Regression:** Do not persist with linear ordinal regression methods, as they failed previously and are ill-suited for the likely non-linear nature of the typological gradations .
*   **Proceeding with Crude PH Features:** Abandon the initial crude PH implementation. The poor signal was likely due to the feature representation, not the absence of topological information. A proper persistence image pipeline is required to unlock its potential .
*   **Reporting CV Accuracy:** Cease reporting the standard 86.1% cross-validation accuracy. It is a misleading metric in this context and undermines trust in the model's true capabilities. The LOGO-CV score is the only valid headline number.

By following this strategic roadmap, the project can transition from a model that is an OAP specialist and a general failure to a robust, honest, and reliable tool for lithic typology classification across diverse archaeological assemblages.
