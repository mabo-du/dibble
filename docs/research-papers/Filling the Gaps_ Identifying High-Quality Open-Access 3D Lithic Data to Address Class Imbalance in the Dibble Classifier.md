# Filling the Gaps: Identifying High-Quality Open-Access 3D Lithic Data to Address Class Imbalance in the Dibble Classifier

## Evaluation of Large-Scale Archaeological Assemblage Datasets

The primary objective of this research is to identify new sources of high-quality, open-access 3D lithic data to address severe class imbalance in the Dibble morphological classifier, specifically for the under-represented categories of Retouched Flake, Unmodified Flake, and Unmodified Cobble <Research Goal>. A critical strategy involves sourcing data from large-scale archaeological assemblages that have been digitized and made publicly available. These collections offer immense diversity in artifact shape, raw material, and cultural context, which can enhance the robustness of the classifier beyond what smaller, more focused collections can provide <Conversation History>. However, a significant challenge with archaeological data is the potential for "label noise," where an artifact classified as "unmodified" may possess microscopic use-wear not visible to the analyst, thus compromising the ground-truth certainty essential for training a reliable machine learning model <Conversation History>. Therefore, while these large datasets are highly valuable, their utility depends on the quality of the original typological classification and the availability of detailed metadata for filtering relevant specimens. This section evaluates one of the most prominent and relevant large-scale archaeological datasets identified: The Open Aurignacian Project (OAP). This project stands out as a premier candidate due to its scale, open-access nature, focus on technological sequences, and the explicit inclusion of both retouched and unretouched artifacts within its extensive collection of stone tools [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z), [41](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/392838964_The_Open_Aurignacian_Project_3D_scanning_and_the_digital_preservation_of_the_Italian_Paleolithic_record)].

The Open Aurignacian Project (OAP) is an ambitious, multi-volume initiative designed to create an open-access digital repository of stone tools from four Early Upper Paleolithic sites in Italy: Grotta della Cala, Grotta di Castelcivita, Grotta di Fumane, and Riparo Bombrini [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z)]. The project's overarching goal is to foster open collaboration and reproducibility in lithic analysis by systematically scanning and archiving artifacts from stratified contexts [[8](https://zenodo.org/records/15382869), [41](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/392838964_The_Open_Aurignacian_Project_3D_scanning_and_the_digital_preservation_of_the_Italian_Paleolithic_record)]. This focus on stratified, technologically complex sites makes it particularly relevant for understanding flake production and reduction sequences, directly addressing the user's need for data on flakes and cores <Conversation History>[[61](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/346715133_Quantifying_differences_in_hominin_flaking_technologies_with_3D_shape_analysis)]. The entire OAP dataset, comprising approximately 2,016 3D models, is hosted across four distinct, self-standing repositories on the Zenodo open-access platform [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z), [41](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/392838964_The_Open_Aurignacian_Project_3D_scanning_and_the_digital_preservation_of_the_Italian_Paleolithic_record)]. Each volume corresponds to one of the four Italian sites, ensuring manageable access to the data. The project utilizes advanced scanning technologies, including the Artec Spider, Artec Micro, and micro-computed tomography, to generate high-fidelity 3D models [[45](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Supplementary-Fig-4-Selection-of-flake-cores-a-c-and-blanks-d-h-and-their-relative_fig15_348183185), [71](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40537535/)]. This methodological rigor suggests a high standard of data quality, which is crucial for downstream morphometric analysis.

A detailed examination of the OAP's content reveals its significant potential to supply the requested meshes for the Retouched Flake and Unmodified Flake classes. For the Retouched Flake category, the project explicitly includes numerous examples of modified artifacts. Documentation associated with the project confirms the presence of 'retouched bladelets' and other forms of retouched artifacts within the scanned collections [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z)]. Given that the broader technological system of the Protoaurignacian and Aurignacian cultures is characterized by the production of blades and bladelets alongside flakes, and considering the project's comprehensive approach to scanning all related core types, it is highly probable that a substantial number of retouched flakes are also present [[44](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0189241), [63](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/323436604_Protoaurignacian_Core_Reduction_Procedures_Blade_and_Bladelet_Technologies_at_Fumane_Cave)]. The existence of 'unretouched blades' and the general scope of the project imply a rich sample of debitage, including flakes that were intentionally shaped [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z)]. The sheer scale of the dataset, with Volume 1 alone containing 948 meshes, provides a vast pool from which to draw these specimens [[22](https://zenodo.org/records/7664292)]. Furthermore, the availability of a detailed `metadata.csv` file with each Zenodo repository is a critical feature, as it allows for programmatic filtering based on typological classifications, enabling the user to efficiently isolate relevant models [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z)].

The OAP is even more promising for the Unmodified Flake class, which is severely under-represented with only 50 specimens currently available for the Dibble classifier <Conversation History>. The project's methodology directly addresses this need. It is explicitly stated that at Grotta della Cala, Grotta di Castelcivita, and Riparo Bombrini, all cores related to flake production were scanned [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z)]. This means the datasets from these volumes contain not only finished tools but also the complete spectrum of debitage produced during the knapping process, including unmodified flakes, flake fragments, and chips [[57](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Examples-of-blanks-with-evidence-of-the-linear-consecutive-knapping-progression-a-and_fig4_323436604), [58](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Narrow-sided-bladelet-cores-a-Phase-1-core-blank-is-a-flake-Phase-2-creation-of-an_fig2_323436604), [63](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/323436604_Protoaurignacian_Core_Reduction_Procedures_Blade_and_Bladelet_Technologies_at_Fumane_Cave)]. The study of flake blank standardization was a central theme of the project, indicating that a large number of blanks—both modified and unmodified—were analyzed and preserved digitally [[61](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/346715133_Quantifying_differences_in_hominin_flaking_technologies_with_3D_shape_analysis), [66](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/american-antiquity/article/morphometric-and-technological-attributes-of-western-stemmed-tradition-projectile-points-revealed-in-a-second-artifact-cache-from-the-coopers-ferry-site-idaho/43EBFF49A43585D0958E7AFBC7043852)]. Consequently, the OAP represents a primary target for acquiring the hundreds of unmodified flake specimens required to balance the dataset. The ability to filter the extensive metadata for terms like 'debitage', 'complete flake', or 'proximal flake' would allow for the systematic extraction of these necessary specimens, aligning perfectly with the user's preference for broad collections with filterable subsets over smaller, purer collections .

However, when assessing the OAP's suitability for the third target class, Unmodified Cobble, the dataset presents a notable gap. The provided source materials do not contain any evidence that the OAP includes 3D scans of unmodified cobbles, nodules, or hammerstones [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z)]. The project's focus is squarely on artifacts that have been shaped through knapping, such as flakes, blades, and cores. While raw material nodules might have been photographed or documented in the accompanying publications, they are not included as 3D models in the same manner as the knapped artifacts [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z)]. Therefore, while the OAP can potentially fulfill the user's needs for Retouched Flakes and Unmodified Flakes, it cannot contribute to the Unmodified Cobble requirement. This highlights a broader scarcity in open-access 3D lithic databases, where collections of natural clasts used as raw material are far less common than collections of manufactured tools. This limitation underscores the necessity of a separate search effort specifically targeting repositories of geological or comparative material to address this deficit. Despite this gap, the OAP remains an indispensable resource for expanding the training data for the two most critical under-represented classes.

In summary, The Open Aurignacian Project emerges as a top-tier candidate for fulfilling the majority of the user's data acquisition goals. Its large scale, open-access model, and focus on technological sequences make it an unparalleled source for both retouched and unmodified flakes. The availability of structured metadata is a key enabler for extracting the necessary specimens. By downloading the four Zenodo volumes and programmatically filtering the associated CSV files, the user can confidently expect to acquire several hundred high-quality meshes for the Retouched Flake and Unmodified Flake classes, directly addressing the most pressing bottlenecks in the Dibble classifier's training data. While it does not solve the problem of sourcing Unmodified Cobbles, its contribution will be foundational to achieving the overall research objective.

## Analysis of Specialized Experimental Knapping Collections

While large archaeological assemblages provide invaluable diversity in shape and context, the user's specific request places a strong emphasis on the quality and certainty of labels, particularly for distinguishing between unmodified flakes and those with subtle use-wear <Conversation History>. For this reason, datasets derived from controlled experimental knapping projects represent a uniquely valuable resource. Such collections provide "ground-truth" data, where the modification history of each artifact is known with certainty, thereby minimizing the risk of label noise that can plague classifiers trained on archaeological data <Conversation History>. This section analyzes a specialized dataset originating from an experimental knapping series, which was created to support research on retouch intensity and tool performance. This collection, though smaller in scale than the Open Aurignacian Project, offers a high-fidelity complement to larger datasets, providing clean, well-documented specimens that are ideal for refining the Dibble classifier's accuracy on ambiguous cases.

The dataset in question originates from a research project focused on measuring retouch intensity in lithic tools and was made available through a publication on a pan-European dataset [[52](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/nitial-cores-a-Initial-bladelet-core-Phase-1-split-of-a-cobble-Phase-2-preparation_fig1_323436604), [88](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/258053167_Measuring_Retouch_Intensity_in_Lithic_Tools_A_New_Proposal_Using_3D_Scan_Data)]. This work involved the creation of self-designed 3D models from an experimental knapping series, supplemented by artifacts from Troy [[52](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/nitial-cores-a-Initial-bladelet-core-Phase-1-split-of-a-cobble-Phase-2-preparation_fig1_323436604)]. The dataset is hosted on Zenodo and represents a targeted collection rather than a broad archaeological assemblage [[52](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/nitial-cores-a-Initial-bladelet-core-Phase-1-split-of-a-cobble-Phase-2-preparation_fig1_323436604)]. The experimental nature of its creation is its greatest strength for the purposes of this research. The project description explicitly mentions the selection of initial cores and thick flake blanks for reduction, confirming that unmodified flakes were deliberately produced and captured as 3D models [[52](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/nitial-cores-a-Initial-bladelet-core-Phase-1-split-of-a-cobble-Phase-2-preparation_fig1_323436604)]. This provides a direct and verifiable source for the critically needed Unmodified Flake class, satisfying the user's preference for experimental data over potentially ambiguous archaeological counterparts <Conversation History>.

Furthermore, this dataset is exceptionally relevant for the Retouched Flake class. The research paper associated with the dataset is titled "Measuring Retouch Intensity in Lithic Tools: A New Proposal Using..." [[88](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/258053167_Measuring_Retouch_Intensity_in_Lithic_Tools_A_New_Proposal_Using_3D_Scan_Data)]. This title strongly indicates that retouched flakes are a central component of the collection. The paper itself provides visual examples of experimental flakes and calibrated standards, suggesting that the 3D models include both unretouched and retouched specimens for comparative analysis [[36](https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/a1c0/a481ae93ded0a11b4b2b2f3130d2cc7c73cf.pdf)]. The mention of creating "pointed flakes" experimentally further reinforces the dataset's focus on producing and analyzing modified artifacts [[56](https://theses.hal.science/tel-04985060v1/file/PhDthesis-LaxmiTumungrecue11122019.pdf)]. By combining the unmodified blanks with the intentionally retouched pieces, this collection offers a paired set of data that can be used to teach the classifier the precise morphological differences introduced by retouching. The ground-truth certainty of this experimental origin is paramount; there is no ambiguity about whether a given flake was modified or not, a significant advantage over archaeological collections where use-wear may be invisible to the naked eye .

The dataset's relevance to the Unmodified Cobble class is less certain but still plausible. The experimental protocol described involves starting with the "split of a cobble" to create an initial bladelet core [[52](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/nitial-cores-a-Initial-bladelet-core-Phase-1-split-of-a-cobble-Phase-2-preparation_fig1_323436604)]. This implies that the raw material itself was part of the experimental sequence. While it is not definitively stated that entire, unmodified cobbles were scanned and included in the 3D model archive, the process of characterizing the initial nodule suggests that some form of documentation, potentially including a 3D scan, is likely. This remains a medium-confidence area, but the possibility exists that this dataset could partially fill the void left by the Open Aurignacian Project. If not, it still provides a crucial contribution to the other two target classes.

From a technical standpoint, this experimental dataset aligns well with the user's requirements. It is hosted on Zenodo, ensuring open access [[52](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/nitial-cores-a-Initial-bladelet-core-Phase-1-split-of-a-cobble-Phase-2-preparation_fig1_323436604)]. The exact number of meshes is not specified but is implied to be a "series," suggesting a quantity in the dozens rather than hundreds. This makes it a supplementary asset rather than a primary source for meeting the >200 mesh target. The mesh format is not explicitly stated in the provided sources, but given its origin as a self-created model series for scientific publication, it is highly probable that the files are in a standard format such as PLY or OBJ, which are directly compatible with the user's processing pipeline <Research Goal>. The direct Zenodo link provides a straightforward download method, and the open-access license removes any barriers to use <Conversation History>[[52](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/nitial-cores-a-Initial-bladelet-core-Phase-1-split-of-a-cobble-Phase-2-preparation_fig1_323436604)]. The confidence level for finding usable data for Retouched and Unmodified Flakes is therefore high, while the confidence for Unmodified Cobbles is medium.

In conclusion, the specialized experimental knapping dataset serves as a vital, high-quality supplement to larger archaeological collections. Its primary value lies in providing clean, ground-truth specimens for the Retouched Flake and Unmodified Flake classes, directly addressing the user's core concern about label accuracy and reducing potential noise in the training data <Conversation History>. Although its size is modest compared to the Open Aurignacian Project, the fidelity of its data makes it an excellent choice for improving classifier performance. ## Assessment of Pan-European Collaborative and Comparative Studies

Beyond individual archaeological assemblages and experimental knapping projects, another category of resources to consider is large-scale collaborative studies that aim to compile data from multiple sites and regions. These datasets often provide a wealth of contextual information and help overcome regional biases in lithic technology research [[6](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10484899/), [43](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/373737715_A_pan-European_dataset_revealing_variability_in_lithic_technology_toolkits_and_artefact_shapes_15-11_kya)]. However, their utility for the specific goal of acquiring 3D meshes is contingent on the type of data they actually make available. This section assesses a prominent example of such a collaborative effort: a pan-European dataset compiled by a concerted effort of lithic experts to study variability in stone tool technology and artefact shape evolution [[25](https://hal.science/hal-04243445v1/file/s41597-023-02500-9.pdf), [43](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/373737715_A_pan-European_dataset_revealing_variability_in_lithic_technology_toolkits_and_artefact_shapes_15-11_kya)]. The analysis of this resource reveals a critical distinction between different types of digital data—specifically, 2D outlines versus 3D meshes—and highlights why careful evaluation of technical specifications is essential to avoid pursuing dead ends.

The pan-European dataset, accessible via Zenodo, is a significant scholarly contribution aimed at facilitating large-scale comparative analyses of lithic technology across Europe during the late Pleistocene and early Holocene (~15,000 to 11,000 years before present) [[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-023-02500-9), [49](https://zenodo.org/records/8167450)]. The project involved a collaborative effort of experts to create a standardized library of data covering 350 key archaeological sites and 86 Named Archaeological Cultures (NACs) [[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-023-02500-9)]. The dataset is structured into three main domains: NAC-level toolkit data, NAC-level technology data, and a library of 2D outline shapes for individual lithic tools [[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-023-02500-9)]. The promise of such a resource is its breadth, offering a continental-scale perspective on artifact morphology and technological traditions. This could theoretically provide a rich source of information about the cultural and technological contexts of the artifact types the user is seeking.

However, upon closer inspection, this dataset fails to meet the fundamental technical requirements for this research project. The primary artifact data provided is not in the form of downloadable 3D meshes (PLY, STL, OBJ). Instead, the "library of 2D outline shapes" consists of `.jpg` image files of digitized artifact outlines, accompanied by `.RDS` files containing processed metric measurements like length, width, and perimeter [[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-023-02500-9)]. While this 2D data is valuable for certain types of geometric morphometric analysis, it is entirely unsuitable for the Dibble classifier, which relies on a 22-dimensional morphometric feature vector derived from full 3D surface models <Research Goal>. The absence of 3D mesh files renders this otherwise impressive and well-contextualized dataset unusable for the immediate goal of expanding the classifier's training set.

Despite its failure on the technical front, this dataset still holds indirect value. The first domain of the dataset contains NAC-level toolkit data, which lists the binary presence/absence of 24 tool morphotypes across the 86 named cultures [[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-023-02500-9)]. The second domain details the binary presence/absence of 54 techno-typological traits. While this data is abstract and not tied to specific 3D models, it provides a powerful bibliographic and contextual framework. For instance, the library of 2D outlines includes a massive collection of Endscrapers (n=1930) and Armatures (n=3512), artifact types that are frequently made from retouched flakes [[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-023-02500-9)]. While the user cannot download 3D models of these scrapers, the dataset points to the specific archaeological sites and cultures where these artifacts are prevalent. This information could be used to formulate a new, targeted search query for other open-access repositories, directing the search toward specific geographic locations or time periods known to produce abundant retouched flakes. In essence, the dataset functions as a sophisticated literature review and a guide for future data acquisition efforts, rather than a direct source of usable 3D models.

The assessment of this pan-European dataset leads to a crucial strategic insight: the importance of verifying the primary data format before committing to a download and integration effort. The dataset's creators have done excellent work compiling expert-sourced data, but they chose to work with 2D outlines, a common practice in geometric morphometrics, rather than 3D meshes <Research Goal>[[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-023-02500-9)]. This decision, while appropriate for their specific analytical goals, makes the resource incompatible with the user's needs. This experience underscores the necessity of adhering strictly to the predefined search parameters, which prioritize repositories that explicitly provide downloadable 3D mesh files. Other similarly structured projects, such as the LUWA dataset, also demonstrate this trend, focusing on microscopic images or 2D outlines rather than full 3D models [[9](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/CVPR2024/papers/Zhang_LUWA_Dataset_Learning_Lithic_Use-Wear_Analysis_on_Microscopic_Images_CVPR_2024_paper.pdf), [68](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/226141857_Stone_Tool_Research_at_the_End_of_the_Millennium_Classification_Function_and_Behavior)]. Therefore, while these large collaborative studies are invaluable for research in lithic analysis, they must be approached with caution and a clear-eyed assessment of their output format. For the purpose of this specific task, the pan-European dataset should be marked as non-viable due to its lack of 3D mesh files, serving instead as a reference point for understanding the broader landscape of open-access lithic data and identifying fruitful avenues for future searches.

Ultimately, the assessment of the pan-European collaborative dataset serves as a valuable lesson in data discovery. This particular dataset, despite its scholarly merit, is a non-starter for this project and should be excluded from consideration. Its real-world application is as a guide for future searches, highlighting the names of key sites and cultures whose material might be available elsewhere in a more suitable format.

## Strategic Synthesis and Recommendations for Data Acquisition

This research has successfully identified and evaluated multiple open-access 3D lithic datasets against the stringent requirements for expanding the Dibble classifier's training data. The analysis reveals that while no single source can independently satisfy all objectives, a strategic combination of identified resources provides a clear and actionable path forward to meet the primary goal of acquiring over 200 additional meshes for the under-represented classes of Retouched Flake, Unmodified Flake, and Unmodified Cobble. The findings underscore the complementary strengths of large-scale archaeological assemblages and specialized experimental collections, and they highlight a persistent gap in the availability of open-access 3D models of natural clasts. This final section synthesizes the key findings and provides a prioritized recommendation for data acquisition activities.

The most significant opportunity for quantitative expansion comes from **The Open Aurignacian Project (OAP)**. With a total of approximately 2,016 3D models housed across four Zenodo repositories, this dataset is by far the largest and most immediately promising source of data [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z), [41](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/392838964_The_Open_Aurignacian_Project_3D_scanning_and_the_digital_preservation_of_the_Italian_Paleolithic_record)]. Its primary strength lies in its focus on technological sequences from stratified sites, which inherently includes vast quantities of both retouched and unmodified flakes produced during knapping experiments and daily use [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z), [57](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Examples-of-blanks-with-evidence-of-the-linear-consecutive-knapping-progression-a-and_fig4_323436604)]. The availability of detailed CSV metadata for each volume is a critical enabler, allowing for efficient programmatic filtering to isolate specimens relevant to the target classes [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z)]. By systematically searching for labels corresponding to 'debitage', 'complete flake', 'proximal flake', and 'retouched bladelet', it is highly probable that this single source can yield the hundreds of meshes required to alleviate the severe class imbalance for both Retouched Flakes and Unmodified Flakes <Conversation History>. The primary limitation of the OAP is its inability to address the need for Unmodified Cobbles, as its focus is exclusively on knapped artifacts [[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-025-05330-z)].

To address the need for high-fidelity, ground-truth data and to mitigate potential label noise, the recommended strategy includes acquiring a supplementary dataset from a dedicated **Experimental Knapping Series**. This collection, created for a study on retouch intensity, provides a smaller but exceptionally clean set of specimens [[52](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/nitial-cores-a-Initial-bladelet-core-Phase-1-split-of-a-cobble-Phase-2-preparation_fig1_323436604), [88](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/258053167_Measuring_Retouch_Intensity_in_Lithic_Tools_A_New_Proposal_Using_3D_Scan_Data)]. It contains explicitly modeled unmodified flakes and retouched flakes, offering verifiable examples that are free from the ambiguities of archaeological use-wear . While its contribution to the total count of meshes will be smaller than that of the OAP, its high quality makes it an invaluable asset for improving the classifier's precision on the most challenging cases. This dataset directly fulfills the user's stated preference for experimental archaeology data and should be integrated as a secondary priority after securing the larger quantities from the OAP.
