# **Automated Stone Tool Typology: Training Data Acquisition, Dataset Design, and Classification Architecture**

## **The Digital Landscape of 3D Lithic Repositories**

The foundational requirement for automating lithic typological classification is the assembly of a highly robust, representative corpus of training data. Because the classification architecture of the Dibble desktop application relies on tabular morphometric features extracted automatically from 3D meshes rather than raw voxel or point-cloud deep learning processing, the data acquisition strategy must evaluate both the availability of raw 3D geometry and the presence of high-quality typological metadata. A systematic survey of the current digital archaeological landscape reveals a highly fragmented repository ecosystem characterized by varying data formats, inconsistent metadata standards, and differing degrees of programmatic accessibility.  
An exhaustive evaluation of the major digital repositories provides a clear inventory of the assets currently available to bootstrap a Random Forest classification model. The utility of these repositories is dictated by their capacity to provide bulk access to dimensionally accurate meshes coupled with expert-validated typological labels.

Zenodo currently represents the most valuable immediate resource for 3D lithic training data. Driven by recent mandates for open science in paleoanthropology, researchers have utilized Zenodo to deposit comprehensive, research-grade datasets that perfectly align with the requirements of automated morphometric extraction. Most notably, the *Open Aurignacian Project* (Volumes 1 and 2\) provides 948 and 732 highly detailed 3D meshes of Early Upper Palaeolithic lithics, specifically partitioned into blades, bladelets, cores, flakes, and retouched tools, sourced from Fumane Cave and Castelcivita.1 Furthermore, the NUBIAN dataset provides comprehensive 3D geometric data for Nubian Levallois cores across Africa and the Levant.5 These datasets provide the immediate, high-fidelity ground truth necessary to initialize a classifier.  
MorphoSource operates as the leading open-access 3D specimen repository for biological specimens, currently hosting over 65,000 physical objects.7 While its archaeological lithic holdings are expanding—such as the Tvarožná X dataset containing 187 retouched tools and cores 8—the metadata architecture of MorphoSource is heavily skewed toward biological taxonomy and stratigraphic provenience rather than fine-grained lithic typology. The platform does provide API access, but retrieving bulk datasets requires programmatic navigation of their complex media folder hierarchy, and many archaeological deposits lack the explicit typological tags required for supervised learning.9  
OpenContext excels in the publication of structured, linked archaeological data.10 While 3D media, such as OBJ files, are present 12, they represent a minor fraction of the total holdings. However, OpenContext is deeply integrated with the Digital Index of North American Archaeology (DINAA), providing stable URIs and machine-readable APIs that make its vast tabular morphometric data highly suitable for supplementing synthetic feature tables, even where the parent 3D mesh is absent.14  
The Digital Archaeological Record (tDAR) operates as a primary repository for digital archaeological data, but access is often gated behind registration walls, and bulk programmatic retrieval is historically constrained by embargoed data and non-standardized typological coding across isolated projects. Synthesizing a cohesive training corpus from tDAR would require a massive, manual ontological harmonization effort that exceeds the capacity of a single open-source developer.  
The Smithsonian 3D Digitization project released millions of digital assets into the public domain (CC0) in 2020, supported by a robust JSON-based API and the open-source Voyager 3D explorer suite.15 However, an analysis of the lithic artifacts hosted on this platform reveals a profound bias toward North American display-quality specimens, such as highly symmetrical Clovis projectile points.20 These artifacts represent the extreme tail of the morphological distribution and are not representative of the ubiquitous flake, core, and amorphous debitage that dominates typical archaeological assemblages. Training a general-purpose classifier on museum-grade display specimens would severely cripple its ability to generalize to standard field data.  
Sketchfab contains thousands of community-uploaded lithic models. By leveraging the Sketchfab API, it is possible to filter exclusively for Creative Commons licenses (CC-BY and CC0) while explicitly excluding non-commercial (NC) and no-derivatives (ND) clauses.22 Despite the high volume, the lack of rigorous peer-reviewed metadata renders Sketchfab largely unusable for supervised learning ground truth. Furthermore, models on Sketchfab are frequently decimated to optimize WebGL rendering performance, stripping them of the geometric fidelity required for automated feature extraction.24 Sketchfab is best viewed as a repository for unsupervised pre-training or data augmentation, rather than a primary source of labelled specimens.

### **Geometric Fidelity and Mesh Quality Thresholds**

Not all hosted meshes possess the geometric resolution required for the extraction of reliable morphometric features. The viability of a 3D mesh depends entirely on its capacity to accurately represent sharp topological boundaries. Extracting the exterior platform angle (EPA) requires the presence of distinct, mathematically planar geometric faces, while automated scar counting requires the clear definition of arrises (dorsal ridges).  
Recent methodological shifts in the discipline have formalized these exact requirements. The *StyroStone protocol*, heavily utilized for the high-quality datasets deposited on Zenodo, establishes that micro-computed tomography (micro-CT) or high-resolution structured light scanning (e.g., Artec Space Spider) is mandatory for small implements like bladelets and microflakes.4 Standard photogrammetry, while highly accessible to field archaeologists, frequently fails to resolve the acute edge angles and translucent boundaries typical of fine-grained flint and obsidian.26  
For reliable extraction of morphometric features, a minimum polygon count is generally dictated by the physical size of the artifact and the spatial sampling theorem. To reliably detect a dorsal scar ridge, the mesh resolution must capture geometric variations at the sub-millimeter level. Models that have been decimated below 50,000 polygons typically exhibit artificially smoothed arrises. This smoothing will cause the automated watershed segmentation algorithms—which rely on discrete curvature estimation schemas applied to mesh vertices 28—to catastrophically undercount dorsal scars and miscalculate edge angles. Therefore, pre-screening algorithms must be implemented to reject any candidate mesh file from a repository where the average edge length of the constituent triangles exceeds 0.5 millimeters, evaluating the surface-area-to-polygon ratio prior to initiating a full volumetric download.

### **License Specifics and Machine Learning Provenance**

The legal landscape regarding machine learning training on CC-BY 4.0 data in 2026 remains highly complex, with intense debate regarding whether the mathematical weights of a trained Random Forest model constitute a "derivative work" of the tabular features extracted from CC-BY meshes.  
Current open-source legal interpretation and community consensus hold that the trained model parameters (specifically, the decision tree split thresholds in a Random Forest) are abstract statistical facts learned from the data, not copyrightable expressions of the original data itself. However, to strictly honor the spirit and letter of CC-BY 4.0, attribution to the original creators must be meticulously maintained. If a model is granted a CC-BY license, the attribution condition requires credit to the model authors and the underlying data creators upon distribution.29  
The introduction of the OpenMDW (Open Model, Data and Weights) license by the Linux Foundation explicitly addresses this exact paradigm.30 Under OpenMDW, models trained on open data can be distributed freely, provided the origin notices of the training data are preserved in the distribution bundle. Crucially, OpenMDW explicitly states that the outputs generated by the model—such as the classification predictions applied to a user's novel artifact—are free from attribution requirements or restrictive copyleft clauses.30 Therefore, the safest and most scientifically rigorous approach is to distribute the trained Random Forest model under OpenMDW, accompanied by a comprehensive PROVENANCE.md ledger. This ledger must list all Zenodo DOIs, MorphoSource ARKs, and authors whose CC-BY data contributed to the training set, thereby satisfying the attribution requirements of the upstream data without encumbering the downstream user.

## **Typological Labelling and the Ground Truth Problem**

Raw geometric data is scientifically useless for supervised classification without highly reliable typological labels. The selection of a specific typological framework is the most critical constraint on classifier performance. A machine learning model is fundamentally bound by the quality of its training data; it cannot achieve an accuracy that surpasses the inter-analyst reliability of the human experts who generated the labels.

### **Inter-Analyst Reliability as a Hard Accuracy Ceiling**

The archaeological literature presents a sobering assessment of lithic typological consistency. Studies conducting blind tests on inter-analyst correspondence routinely report severe systematic biases and alarming rates of misclassification among human experts. For instance, when evaluating lithic technological types, Cohen's Kappa (![][image1]) scores—a statistical measure of inter-rater agreement—frequently fall into the "poor" to "fair" range.31 A comprehensive study assessing different analysts identifying core flakes versus undetermined flakes reported ![][image1] values as low as 0.07 to 0.54, with retouched tools showing agreement of only 0.36 to 0.59.32  
When assessing qualitative features—such as eraillure scars, cone of percussion, or extraction axis—Fleiss' Kappa measures show significant agreement for almost none of the features, demonstrating that human raters fail to perform significantly better than chance in certain granular assessments.33 If the ground truth data exhibits an inter-analyst reliability of ![][image2], a supervised Random Forest classifier trained on this data will inevitably encode this subjective noise. The theoretical maximum accuracy of the model is fundamentally capped by this label variance; a classifier cannot reliably predict a nuanced typological category that human experts themselves cannot consistently define or agree upon.

### **Typological Framework Selection**

Given these severe epistemological and statistical constraints, highly granular classification schemes are computationally intractable for a generalizable open-source tool. The following frameworks present distinct challenges:

1. **Bordes Typology**: Comprising 92 distinct morphological types for the Middle Palaeolithic, this system is notorious for poor inter-analyst reliability and its reliance on highly subjective boundaries between continuous morphological variations. Sufficient, consistently labelled 3D data across all 92 classes simply does not exist in the open domain.  
2. **Dibble-McPherron Reduction Stage Typology**: Grounded in the EPA-PD (Exterior Platform Angle \- Platform Depth) model, this framework focuses on the intensity of flaking rather than abstract morphology.34 While mechanically robust and highly replicable, labels for reduction stages are highly interpretive and are rarely published as standard metadata in broad open-access repositories.  
3. **Tixier / Inizan Technological Classification**: Focused on the operational sequence (chaîne opératoire), this is excellent for overarching narrative analysis but relies on abstract sequences (e.g., initial, centripetal, predetermined) that are extraordinarily difficult to infer from isolated end-products without extensive physical refitting.36  
4. **Broad Technological Categories**: Classifying artifacts as Core, Flake, Blade, Bladelet, or Retouched Tool represents the coarsest possible classification, but it is the most statistically tractable.

Based on the available labelled data landscape and the constraints of inter-analyst reliability, the classifier must be exclusively trained on **Broad Technological Categories**. The available high-quality datasets, such as the Open Aurignacian Project, are already explicitly partitioned into these broad categories.1 Inter-analyst agreement for these broad morphological and technological classes is substantially higher (![][image3]) than for finer typologies.32 By restricting the initial classification space to these fundamental categories, the Random Forest model stands a realistic chance of achieving \>85% accuracy, providing a mathematically reliable baseline upon which users can subsequently build their own finer-grained, subjective typological analyses.

## **Journal Supplementary Data and the Literature Extraction Dilemma**

Where raw 3D meshes are unavailable, tabular morphometric data extracted from published literature can theoretically serve as direct input for training the Random Forest classifier. Many lithic analysis papers publish supplementary data files, representing a potentially rich source of labelled feature tables.

### **Geometric Morphometrics (GMM) vs. Technological Morphometrics**

The literature is heavily populated with Geometric Morphometrics (GMM) datasets, such as those documenting Acheulean handaxes or Levallois cores using 3D Procrustes shape coordinates.6 However, GMM landmark data defines shape in a multidimensional Principal Component Analysis (PCA) space through homologous landmark placement. This approach is fundamentally orthogonal to the discrete technological measurements—such as edge angle in degrees, absolute mass in grams, and exact dorsal scar counts—extracted by standard morphometric pipelines.39  
While GMM is exceptional for tracking continuous shape variation and evolutionary drift, it cannot be mapped directly onto Dibble's discrete technological features. Raw landmark coordinate datasets (.nts, .pts) cannot be ingested as training features for a classifier relying on metric dimensions and angles, rendering a vast swath of published GMM supplementary data mathematically incompatible with the current extraction architecture.

### **Systematic Data Extraction from PDF Literature**

To augment the training dataset, one might attempt to systematically extract tabular data from PDF supplementary materials in journals such as *PLOS ONE*, *Journal of Archaeological Science*, and *Lithic Technology*. However, the programmatic extraction of tabular data from PDFs is notoriously brittle.  
Archaeological tables suffer from highly variable column counts, summary rows that break table structures, and modern borderless aesthetic layouts.40 An analysis of Python PDF extraction libraries reveals severe limitations:

* **pdfplumber**: Provides pixel-level control and visual debugging, excelling at complex, ambiguous layouts, but requires significant manual tuning per individual document.40  
* **Camelot**: Utilizes lattice and stream detection. It is highly effective for well-bordered tables but completely fails on modern borderless designs commonly found in supplementary PDFs.41  
* **Deep Learning Solutions (TATR / Docling)**: Transformer-based Table-Detection models (Table Transformer \- TATR) have recently demonstrated superior versatility, achieving higher recall and accuracy (\~93%) across diverse scientific documents compared to rule-based tools like Tabula (67.9%) and Camelot (73.0%).42

Despite the advancements in transformer-based extraction, attempting manual or programmatic extraction of supplementary tables across hundreds of disparate PDF formats is a highly inefficient use of single-developer resources. The realistic time cost is estimated at over 150 researcher-hours to manually extract, clean, and standardize a labelled dataset of 2,000 specimens from published PDFs. This effort massively outweighs the benefit, given the presence of pre-compiled, pristine CSV metadata in repositories like Zenodo. Therefore, data acquisition should be strictly limited to API-accessible, native CSV/Excel supplementary files, explicitly avoiding PDF extraction.

## **Existing Labelled Collections and Data Analogues**

A comprehensive review of existing data silos highlights a few key resources and important precedents from adjacent scientific fields that dictate how the data collection strategy should proceed.

### **Primary Lithic Collections**

* **The Open Aurignacian Project**: As established, this is the gold standard for the specific use case of 3D tabular extraction. It provides nearly 1,700 3D meshes of Early Upper Palaeolithic lithics with associated CSV metadata classifying them into core, blade, bladelet, flake, and retouched tool categories.1  
* **ROCEEH Out of Africa Database (ROAD)**: A massive geo-relational database integrating archaeological assemblages spanning three million years. While it lacks the raw 3D meshes, it holds extensive tabular records on raw material, typology, and technological attributes for tens of thousands of lithics, accessible via an R package API (roadDB).44 This is the premier source for establishing statistical metric ranges for synthetic data generation.  
* **Lithics3D R Package**: Developed by Royer and Pop, this package focuses on high-level functions for the semi-automatic segmentation of meshes (e.g., measuring edge angles and thickness).28 However, it is an algorithmic toolkit rather than a repository; it does not bundle a massive corpus of pre-labelled 3D training data.

### **Analogues from Adjacent Fields: Endless Forams**

The most direct analogue to the current lithic classification problem is the *Endless Forams* project from paleoceanography. Researchers faced an identical bottleneck: a lack of resources to train students in discerning closely related microfossil species, exacerbated by a limited number of taxonomic experts.52 They compiled over 34,000 images of planktonic foraminifera, relying on the Zooniverse citizen-science platform to aggregate and validate labels from the community.53 Training a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) on \~27,000 of these verified images yielded an 87.4% classification accuracy.52 This project conclusively demonstrates that a critical mass for reliable image-based morphological machine learning classification lies between 10,000 and 30,000 highly verified instances.

### **The 2D Photographic Stepping Stone**

Given the limitations of 3D data, 2D photographic datasets have been heavily utilized in recent archaeological machine learning. Implementations of Faster R-CNN on 2D lithic images have achieved near-perfect accuracy (100% agreement with human analysts) in the narrow binary task of distinguishing worked stone artifacts from naturally occurring lithic clasts, using a dataset of \~6,700 images.57  
However, there is a profound quality gap between classification based on 2D photos and 3D morphometric features. While 2D CNNs can detect the presence of flaking by analyzing small-scale pixel texture patterns 59, they fundamentally fail to extract true volumetric data, platform angles, and geometric depth. According to the EPA-PD physical model developed by Harold Dibble, exterior platform angle and platform depth are the primary mechanical drivers that dictate the size and shape of a detached flake.34 A 2D image cannot reliably measure a platform angle. Therefore, while 2D CNNs are excellent for binary presence/absence detection, the 3D tabular approach is scientifically superior for typological discrimination because it bases classification on the exact physical and mechanical properties governing stone fracture, rather than surface-level pixel patterns.

## **Synthetic Data Validity and Generation**

Given the limited availability of perfectly labelled 3D meshes across all global lithic traditions (e.g., the severe lack of African MSA/LSA 3D data compared to European Upper Palaeolithic data), generating synthetic tabular data to pre-train the classifier is a statistically valid interim strategy.

### **The Statistical Validity of Synthetic Data**

In archaeological and biological morphometrics, synthetic data generation is a highly recognized technique to overcome sample size limitations. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) have been successfully employed to produce multidimensional synthetic morphometric data that is statistically indistinguishable from original training data, allowing researchers to augment sparse fossil records and improve predictive visualization.60 Synthetic minority over-sampling techniques (SMOTE) are routinely used to artificially raise the number of samples for underrepresented lithic contact materials in use-wear classification.61

### **The Danger of Independent Gaussian Sampling**

However, generating tabular data by independently sampling from published univariate mean and standard deviation distributions (e.g., pulling a random length, then a random width, then a random platform angle) is highly dangerous and statistically invalid. Lithic morphometrics are governed by the strict physical mechanics of conchoidal fracture. The EPA-PD model dictates that flake mass and length are inherently constrained by the exterior platform angle and the depth of percussion.34 Furthermore, longer flakes tend to have wider platforms, and higher cortex coverage generally correlates with lower dorsal scar counts.  
If synthetic features are generated independently, the resulting dataset will contain physically impossible artifacts—for instance, a massive, heavy flake generated alongside a microscopic platform and an extremely acute angle. A Random Forest trained on these independent distributions will learn decision boundaries that do not exist in physical reality, leading to catastrophic generalization failure when evaluated against real, out-of-distribution (OOD) artifacts.

### **Covariance Structure Preservation**

To generate valid synthetic data, the covariance structure of the features must be rigorously preserved. The generation methodology must proceed as follows:

1. **Sourcing Empirical Matrices**: Covariance matrices for specific broad typologies (flake, blade, core) must be estimated from the raw tabular data available in the ROCEEH ROAD database 48 or the Zenodo CSV supplements.1  
2. **Generation Method**: Instead of independent Gaussian sampling, synthetic tabular rows should be generated using a **Gaussian Copula** or by applying a **Cholesky decomposition** to the empirical covariance matrix. This mathematical transformation ensures that the generated synthetic lithics strictly respect the volumetric correlations, allometric scaling laws, and mechanical constraints of real rock fracture.

The desktop application can ship with an initial synthetic baseline model trained on this covariance-preserved data. However, the user interface must transparently identify this as a "Synthetic Prior Model," explicitly communicating to the archaeologist that it is an interim statistical tool intended to bootstrap initial analyses, not a definitive oracle.

## **Community Data Collection and Quality Control Strategy**

The ultimate, long-term success of an automated, open-source lithic classifier relies on a "Ship First, Learn Later" architecture, transitioning away from the synthetic baseline toward a robust empirical model powered by community contributions.

### **Successful Models from Adjacent Fields: iNaturalist**

The **iNaturalist** platform provides the optimal blueprint for structuring community data collection and label quality control.52 iNaturalist utilizes a highly effective consensus algorithm: new observations begin in a "Needs ID" state. When multiple independent users agree on a taxonomic classification, the observation is automatically elevated to "Research Grade".63  
Crucially, iNaturalist performs no secondary expert verification on Research Grade data before using it to retrain their massive machine learning models.65 The "wisdom of the crowd," structured through mathematical consensus, filters out noise effectively, bypassing the bottleneck of relying on a handful of designated academic experts.

### **Label Quality Control Design for Lithics**

To crowdsource lithic typological labels securely, the software must implement an opt-in data sharing mechanism. When an archaeologist processes an assemblage locally, they can opt to upload the automatically extracted morphometric feature tables (and optionally, heavily decimated 3D meshes) to a central open repository.

1. **Consensus Labelling**: A minimum of three independent classifications (uploaded or verified by different users/institutions) matching the exact same broad technological category should be required to promote a specimen to the "Ground Truth" training corpus.  
2. **Data Privacy and GDPR**: Archaeological site location data is highly sensitive. Disclosing exact spatial coordinates can lead directly to the illicit looting of vulnerable archaeological sites.66 Furthermore, under the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the personal data of the analysts and collectors must be protected.70 The software pipeline must strictly strip all exact spatial coordinates and personally identifiable information, isolating provenience to a broad regional tag (e.g., "Southern France, Middle Palaeolithic") before any telemetry data is transmitted over the network.

## **Existing Machine Learning Classification Work and Baseline Expectations**

Understanding the baseline expectations established by previous machine learning research prevents over-promising on classifier performance and justifies the selection of the Random Forest algorithm over deep learning alternatives.

### **Algorithm Performance: Random Forest Dominance**

A survey of the published literature reveals that Random Forest (RF) algorithms consistently outperform alternative models like Support Vector Machines (SVM), Naïve Bayes, and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) in tabular archaeological classification tasks.73 In a rigorous study of lithic sourcing in Britain, Random Forest achieved the highest classification performance with an 85% average accuracy (measured via F1 Score).74 In highly specific morphometric predictive modeling, Random Forest achieved an exceptional ![][image4] value of 0.97 when predicting original flake mass from successively resharpened retouched tools, completely outperforming competing algorithms.75

### **RF vs. CNN: The Argument for Tabular Features**

While Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) applied to 2D images or 3D voxels can achieve 100% accuracy in highly constrained binary tasks (e.g., worked stone vs. natural clast) 57, their performance degrades dramatically when introduced to real-world noise. Studies demonstrate that if the scanning resolution drops, or if mild weathering processes (represented as Gaussian blur in the image data) obscure small-scale textures, the CNN classification accuracy collapses rapidly toward the level of a random guess.59  
Furthermore, applying 3D deep learning models (like PointNet or VoxNet) to raw lithic meshes is computationally exorbitant, requiring dedicated GPUs that field archaeologists rarely possess. A Random Forest operating on 20+ tabular morphometric features is computationally lightweight, highly resistant to overfitting, and explicitly interpretable. Feature importance scores generated by the Random Forest can be audited directly by archaeologists, confirming that the algorithm is classifying based on valid physical parameters (e.g., mass, EPA, Platform Depth) 34, rather than spurious correlations in the background pixels or voxel noise.

## **Minimum Viable Dataset Design and Release Architecture**

Synthesizing the empirical findings regarding algorithm performance, sample sizing, and data availability, the precise architecture for the classifier and the training data specification can be rigidly defined.

### **Minimum Sample Size and Cross-Validation Strategy**

For a Random Forest classifier processing approximately 20 input features and classifying into 4 to 5 broad technological categories, statistical literature on machine learning sample size estimation provides clear boundaries. To achieve adequate discriminatory ability without severe overfitting, a minimum of 10 to 50 events per variable (EPV) is required.77 Therefore, a minimum viable dataset requires a rule of thumb of roughly **200 to 500 validated specimens per typological class** (totaling between 1,000 and 2,500 rows for a 5-class system). The Open Aurignacian Project datasets alone provide sufficient volume to meet this threshold for flakes and blades.  
**Cross-Validation Strategy**: Standard k-fold cross-validation is mathematically inappropriate for lithic training data. Artifacts from a single site or assemblage share highly correlated raw material properties, specific regional knapping traditions, and identical taphonomic weathering states. If data from the same site is split across the training and testing sets, the model will leak data, artificially inflating accuracy metrics. The evaluation protocol must use **Leave-One-Assemblage-Out (LOAO)** cross-validation.76 This rigorous strategy ensures the model is tested on its ability to generalize to entirely unseen sites, accurately reflecting the true operational use-case of the software when deployed by a new user.

### **Model Serialization Format: ONNX vs. Joblib**

Deploying a trained scikit-learn model within a Python PyQt6 desktop application requires careful consideration of the serialization format.

* **Joblib / Pickle**: This is the native serialization format for scikit-learn and is highly efficient for memory-mapping large NumPy arrays.79 However, pickle executes arbitrary code upon deserialization. This presents an unacceptable security vulnerability for an open-source tool that intends to automatically download updated community-trained models from the web, opening the application to severe supply-chain attacks.80 Furthermore, joblib files are tightly coupled to the specific version of scikit-learn used during training, leading to dependency conflicts.79  
* **ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange)**: ONNX provides a vendor-neutral, highly secure computation graph format.82 ONNX models are highly portable, future-proof across different hardware backends, and strictly immune to arbitrary code execution.81

**Recommendation:** The Random Forest model must be serialized and distributed via **ONNX**. This architecture explicitly decouples the classifier updates from the desktop application's internal Python dependency versions, allowing seamless, versioned model updates in the background without risking user security.

### **Uncertainty Communication: Conformal Prediction**

A critical, well-documented flaw in standard Random Forest classifiers is their tendency to confidently misclassify out-of-distribution (OOD) data. If a user uploads a scanned piece of natural debitage, a modern ceramic shard, or a highly atypical African MSA point to a model trained entirely on European Upper Palaeolithic blades, a standard model will confidently assign an incorrect label.  
To mitigate this, the classifier architecture must implement **Conformal Prediction**. Libraries such as **MAPIE (Model Agnostic Prediction Interval Estimator)** wrap standard scikit-learn estimators to provide mathematically guaranteed prediction intervals and robust out-of-distribution detection, assuming only that the data is exchangeable.83  
Instead of outputting a single, overconfident class label, a conformally predicted Random Forest will output a prediction set (e.g., denoting ambiguity, or an empty set denoting a complete anomaly). If the anomaly set is empty, the software securely informs the user that the artifact falls outside the model's current training distribution.84 This architectural decision fosters trust, ensures scientific rigor, and prevents the software from acting as a flawed "black box" oracle.

## **Final Priority-Ordered Recommendations**

To successfully launch the classification pipeline with minimal resources while ensuring maximum scientific validity, the developer should execute the following three priority actions:

1. **Bootstrap an Empirical Tabular Dataset from Zenodo**: Immediately download the *Open Aurignacian Project* (Volumes 1 & 2\) and the *NUBIAN* datasets from Zenodo and OSF. These repositories provide over 1,500 high-quality, broad-category labels (Flake, Blade, Core, Retouched Tool) with corresponding high-resolution 3D meshes. Process these meshes through the desktop tool's existing extraction pipeline to generate the foundational tabular training matrix. This completely bypasses the mathematical risks associated with synthetic data generation and provides a highly reliable, physically accurate training baseline.  
2. **Implement MAPIE for Conformal Prediction**: Wrap the scikit-learn Random Forest model using the MAPIE library before exporting the final computation graph to ONNX. This is a critical safety measure for an application intended for global archaeologists; the tool must fail gracefully and flag out-of-distribution artifacts rather than confidently outputting incorrect typologies, thereby preserving the scientific integrity of the user's analysis.  
3. **Establish an Opt-In Telemetry Pipeline for Consensus Data**: Build a privacy-first, opt-in feature within the desktop application that allows users to upload their automatically extracted morphometric feature rows (strictly stripped of site coordinates and the heavy 3D mesh geometry) alongside their manual typological corrections. Implement an automated continuous integration pipeline on the server side that aggregates this tabular data, applies a strict 3-user consensus threshold for label validation, and periodically retrains and releases a new ONNX model under the OpenMDW license to the community.
