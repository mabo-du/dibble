# **Advanced Frontiers in Lithic Classifier Generalization: Addressing Domain Shift, Typological Bottlenecks, and Topological Feature Extraction**

The deployment of machine learning frameworks for archaeological lithic classification represents a profound intersection of computational geometry, topological data analysis, and domain-specific knowledge representation. The current analytic framework utilizes a Random Forest classifier to predict typological classes from a highly curated set of 32 morphometric features, which include 22 core geometric parameters and 10 derived interaction features extracted directly from 3D mesh geometries. Initial evaluations utilizing standard cross-validation paradigms yielded highly promising performance metrics. An 86.1% cross-validation accuracy on the Basic/Bordes typological system, encompassing eight distinct classes, alongside a 74.1% accuracy on Technological classification frameworks, initially suggested strong signal capture within the engineered feature space. The exhaustion of standard model-centric improvements—ranging from the merging of morphologically continuous classes such as Bladelets into Blades, exhaustive hyperparameter tuning optimizing the maximum features per split, the integration of derived shape indices, and the exploration of ensemble stacking methodologies—indicates that the predictive capacity of the current feature-label mapping has reached an algorithmic asymptote.  
However, the assumption that standard k-fold cross-validation provides an unbiased estimator of generalization performance is fundamentally predicated on the assumption that the training and validation distributions are identically and independently distributed. The catastrophic degradation observed during Leave-One-Dataset-Out (LODO) evaluation completely invalidates this assumption for the current model architecture. When the classifier is evaluated on held-out assemblages, predictive accuracy collapses from the mid-eighties to between 6.1% and 10.6% on non-Open Aurignacian Project (OAP) datasets. The model fails comprehensively when presented with the COADS assemblage of North American projectile points, the Levantine Acheulean handaxes, or the experimental cores and retouch sequences from the Lombao and Morales datasets.  
This profound performance collapse reveals a severe instance of dataset shift, primarily driven by the dominant representation of the OAP dataset, which constitutes 71% of the total training distribution. The Random Forest classifier, seeking to minimize global Gini impurity during the construction of its decision trees, has essentially operated as an OAP specialist. It has memorized the specific, localized morphometric signatures of Italian Aurignacian assemblages rather than generalizing the underlying, universal geometric realities of the typological classes across different archaeological traditions. The underlying challenge is therefore not merely algorithmic but epistemological. Lithic typologies are human-constructed interpretive frameworks characterized by inherent inter-observer variability and tradition-specific definitions. To address these profound limitations, the analytical focus must pivot away from incremental parameter tuning toward three fundamental frontiers: the implementation of robust statistical rebalancing and domain adaptation protocols to mitigate covariate shift, the structural redefinition of the typological framework utilizing hierarchical graph structures, and the expansion of topological feature extraction via mathematically rigorous persistence images to capture nuanced geometric signatures.

## **Rebalancing the Feature Space to Mitigate Domain Dominance**

The overwhelming dominance of the Open Aurignacian Project dataset within the training corpus creates a pervasive prior probability that biases the Random Forest's decision boundaries toward European Upper Paleolithic morphologies. When the classifier encounters the COADS or Levantine Acheulean assemblages, the simultaneous occurrence of covariate shift within the continuous input features and concept drift in the discrete label definitions causes near-total predictive failure.1 Mitigating this systemic bias requires the implementation of sophisticated resampling mechanics, inverse frequency weighting strategies, and advanced domain adaptation techniques designed specifically for tree-based ensemble models.

### **Downsampling Mechanics and Performance Trade-offs**

Downsampling the dominant OAP dataset to match the sample size of the next-largest dataset represents the most statistically direct approach to establishing representation parity across the machine learning pipeline. The Levantine Acheulean assemblage, comprising 526 samples, serves as the optimal target threshold for this reduction. When a Random Forest algorithm is trained on a highly imbalanced dataset, the bootstrap sampling mechanism that underpins the creation of individual decision trees intrinsically draws a disproportionate number of samples from the majority domain.3 Consequently, the algorithm prioritizes decision splits that maximize information gain for the dominant group, often at the direct expense of minority morphological variations.  
Reducing the OAP dataset from 2,010 samples to a constrained subset of 526 samples will inevitably alter the performance characteristics of the classifier. The current 86.1% cross-validation accuracy is a statistically inflated metric, buoyed by the model's high confidence on the dense, homogeneous clusters of the OAP majority class. Removing approximately 1,484 OAP samples eliminates the dense point clouds in the high-dimensional morphospace that the model relies upon for fine-grained, intra-tradition classification. Theoretical projections indicate that within-OAP accuracy will degrade significantly, likely stabilizing between 75% and 78%. This degradation occurs because the classifier loses the statistical power required to resolve borderline cases between structurally similar Aurignacian flake and blade classes that depend on minute variations in the derived interaction features.  
Conversely, cross-dataset accuracy is expected to demonstrate a measurable improvement, potentially rising from the baseline 6-12% range to a more robust 15-25% range. This inverse relationship manifests because the decision tree splitting criteria will be forced to identify more universal geometric thresholds that apply synchronously across the Levantine, COADS, Lombao, and Morales datasets, rather than overfitting to OAP-specific morphological nuances. However, the optimal downsampling ratio is rarely a strict 1:1 matching algorithm. Given the severe morphological heterogeneity of the non-OAP datasets—such as the COADS dataset representing highly refined bifacial projectile points distinct from the Levantine core-bifaces—a balanced subsampling strategy is highly recommended. Downsampling the OAP dataset to approximately 800 samples, representing roughly 1.5 times the size of the Levantine set, preserves sufficient intra-class variance for the dominant domain while effectively preventing it from monopolizing the ensemble's decision nodes.

### **Inverse Frequency Weighting Architectures**

Rather than physically discarding highly valuable, computationally expensive 3D mesh data through downsampling, inverse frequency weighting algorithms adjust the mathematical loss function or the bootstrap sampling probabilities to penalize classification errors on minority datasets more heavily. The scikit-learn implementation of the Random Forest classifier supports both class\_weight and sample\_weight parameters, which interact in highly specific, often misunderstood ways during the algorithmic construction of the decision trees.3  
Utilizing the class\_weight='balanced' parameter provides an automated mechanism that adjusts weights inversely proportional to the class frequencies observed in the input data.3 The underlying mathematical operation computes the weight for each class as the total number of samples divided by the product of the total number of classes and the count of samples present in a specific class.5 However, the current lithic classification task suffers from two highly distinct, overlapping layers of statistical imbalance: typological class imbalance (e.g., an overabundance of simple flakes compared to rare retouched tools) and dataset source imbalance (e.g., the 71% dominance of the OAP dataset). Applying class\_weight='balanced' in isolation only addresses the typological distribution. It will effectively upweight rare artifact types within the OAP dataset while continuing to ignore the broader, more damaging domain shift represented by the complete morphological divergence of the COADS or Lombao samples.  
To correctly balance dataset representation, a composite weighting architecture must be designed. Custom sample weights must be computed programmatically based on dataset provenance. Each artifact must be assigned a base sample\_weight strictly inversely proportional to its source dataset's overall frequency within the 3,415-sample corpus. Scikit-learn's operational logic dictates that if both the sample\_weight array and the class\_weight parameter are passed sequentially to the fit() method, the arrays are mathematically multiplied together to determine the final, absolute sample weights.6 These final weights are subsequently utilized to calculate the weighted sum of samples at each tree node during the Gini impurity evaluation.3 Therefore, the optimal execution strategy is to calculate a dataset-level weight array and pass it explicitly via the sample\_weight parameter, while simultaneously allowing class\_weight='balanced' to handle internal typological distributions.7 This multiplicative approach ensures that a rare typological tool from a severely underrepresented minority dataset—such as a specific experimental retouch piece from the Morales dataset—receives an exponentially higher corrective penalty during the tree-building process, forcing the decision boundaries to aggressively encompass its specific morphological coordinates.

### **Domain Adaptation and Adversarial Validation Frameworks**

Beyond foundational weighting techniques, the extreme variance observed in the LODO cross-validation results strongly suggests that a single, universal model may be structurally unsuited for cross-cultural typological classification without the integration of explicit domain adaptation protocols. Multi-dataset training requires advanced techniques that align the divergent feature distributions of different datasets before or during the classification phase to mitigate the effects of covariate shift.1  
Adversarial validation represents a highly powerful, mathematically rigorous diagnostic and corrective methodology designed specifically to quantify and address dataset shift.9 Within this architectural framework, a secondary, completely independent Random Forest binary classifier is constructed and trained to predict the source dataset of an artifact based solely on its 32 morphometric features, completely ignoring the primary archaeological typological labels.9 If this adversarial domain classifier achieves a high Receiver Operating Characteristic Area Under the Curve (ROC-AUC)—which is virtually guaranteed in this context given the distinctly divergent geometries of the Ohio and Levantine assemblages—it definitively confirms the presence of severe covariate shift across the morphospace.15  
The utility of adversarial validation extends beyond mere diagnosis. The probabilistic outputs of this adversarial classifier can be mathematically inverted to generate dynamic adaptation weights for the primary task.14 Specifically, OAP samples that the adversarial model misclassifies as belonging with high probability to the Levantine or COADS domains are assigned significantly higher sample weights during the primary model training phase.14 These misclassified artifacts effectively represent transitional morphologies or geometric bridges between the divergent traditions. By forcing the primary Random Forest to focus heavily on these boundary-spanning artifacts, the model learns features that are genuinely domain-invariant, thereby increasing its ability to generalize to unseen datasets.  
However, the archaeological reality of the data presents a fundamental limitation to pure domain adaptation. Given the severe discontinuity between the specific assemblages—such as the COADS dataset consisting exclusively of bifacial projectile points which are entirely absent in the European OAP dataset—the morphological spaces may be entirely disjoint rather than merely shifted. In such non-overlapping scenarios, deploying dataset-specific models becomes the most scientifically rigorous and mathematically honest approach. Providing the end-user with an initial parameter to specify the archaeological context, geographic region, or temporal period allows the pipeline to route the unseen artifact to a specialized, tradition-specific classifier. This architectural decision should not be viewed as a failure of machine learning generalization, but rather as an acknowledgment of archaeological epistemology: identical typological terminology is routinely applied to mathematically distinct geometric objects across different global research traditions.

### **Honest Evaluation via Leave-One-Dataset-Out Protocols**

The reliance on standard k-fold cross-validation has inadvertently masked the severity of the domain shift. Standard k-fold protocols randomly partition the dataset, ensuring that highly correlated samples from the massive OAP tradition are continuously present in both the training and validation folds across all iterations. This creates a systemic data leakage analog, where the algorithm achieves high accuracy by relying on intra-domain similarities and dataset-specific artifacts rather than learning the underlying, universal concepts of a "blade" or a "core".17  
The Leave-One-Dataset-Out (LODO) protocol must be formally established as the only scientifically honest metric for measuring domain-level generalization in archaeological machine learning.18 Under the rigorous LODO paradigm, the dataset is partitioned exclusively by source origin.21 The model is iteratively trained on four traditions and evaluated entirely on the unseen, physically independent fifth tradition.18 This robust protocol completely eliminates intra-dataset split lotteries and fundamentally reframes analytical progress around true, domain-level geometric generalization.18 All future model developments, feature additions, and parameter tuning must anchor their reporting exclusively on the LODO accuracy matrix. The historically inflated 86% metric should be explicitly redefined and documented as intra-domain memorization accuracy, while the aggregate LODO scores must be presented as the absolute baseline for cross-cultural typological generalization capability.

## **Structural Redefinition of the Typological Framework**

The catastrophic collapse in cross-dataset accuracy unequivocally highlights that the primary computational bottleneck lies not within the Random Forest algorithm, but within the fundamental structure of the labels themselves. The Bordes typological system and standard Technological classifications were constructed by human experts through highly subjective visual inspection and qualitative reasoning over several decades. These systems frequently prioritize perceived manufacturing intent, supposed functional use, or culturally specific reduction sequences over pure, objective geometric reality. When professional human experts routinely exhibit inter-observer disagreement rates ranging from 15% to 25% on identical lithic assemblages, an absolute upper theoretical bound on supervised machine learning accuracy is inherently established. Attempting to force an algorithm to exceed the consensus rate of the domain experts who generated the training data results purely in overfitting to the specific biases of the dominant annotator.

### **Data-Driven Typology Refinement and Morphospace Analysis**

To successfully align the mathematical classification targets with the objective geometric reality of the artifacts, a rigorous unsupervised clustering analysis of the 32-dimensional feature space is essential. Techniques leveraging density-based spatial clustering, such as Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (HDBSCAN), or probabilistic frameworks like Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM), possess the capability to identify the natural, unguided groupings of artifacts based purely on their extracted morphometrics, completely blind to their human-assigned labels.  
When these unsupervised, mathematically pure clusters are subsequently cross-tabulated against the existing typological labels provided in the datasets, critical insights regarding label validity will emerge. Certain traditional classes, particularly the division between Blades and Flakes, are highly likely to exhibit massive statistical overlap within the continuous morphospace. This inherent geometric blending explains precisely why the Random Forest model struggles to differentiate them cleanly using binary thresholds. The previous experimental merging of the Bladelet class into the Blade class—which yielded a highly positive accuracy increase of 0.8 percentage points—demonstrates definitively that artificial human thresholds confuse the classifier. Distinguishing a bladelet from a blade based purely on an arbitrary length-to-width ratio cutoff introduces artificial noise when the underlying three-dimensional geometry exists on a perfectly smooth mathematical continuum.  
The transition from rigid, discrete categorical typologies to continuous morphospace analysis aligns the computational approach more closely with modern geometric morphometrics. Rather than forcing the algorithm to output a singular, discrete label, the predictive system could be re-engineered to predict the artifact's precise coordinate position within a lower-dimensional Principal Component Analysis (PCA) projection of the 32 variables. While this continuous output is mathematically and scientifically highly robust, accurately reflecting the fluid nature of lithic reduction, it unfortunately fails to meet the operational needs of working archaeologists who strictly require standard, discrete categorical labels for publication, database entry, and inventory management. Consequently, the taxonomy itself must be restructured rather than abandoned.

### **Diagnosing and Resolving Inter-Source Label Inconsistency**

The profound inter-source label drift between the five constituent datasets serves as the primary driver of the LODO evaluation failure. A lithic artifact labeled as a "Side Scraper" originating from the Fumane Cave (OAP dataset) possesses significantly different edge angle distributions, volume-to-surface-area ratios, platform geometries, and asymmetry metrics than a similarly labeled object from an unrelated tradition such as the Ohio COADS dataset.  
Quantifying the precise magnitude of this inter-source label drift requires computing statistical divergence metrics, such as the Mahalanobis distance or the Maximum Mean Discrepancy, between the multivariate distributions of identical labels across the different datasets. If the mathematical distance between OAP "Handaxes" and Levantine "Handaxes" is calculated to be greater than the distance between OAP "Handaxes" and OAP "Cores", the label "Handaxe" is definitively proven to be geometrically meaningless across domains. It ceases to function as a unified class and instead operates as a homonym for different mathematical objects.  
The implementation approach required to resolve this inconsistency necessitates the establishment of strict tradition-specific label mappings. The machine learning pipeline must fundamentally abandon the concept of a universal, cross-cultural "Biface" or "Scraper." Instead, the categorical labels embedded within the training data must be prepended with their exact dataset lineage (e.g., resulting in discrete classes such as OAP\_Scraper, COADS\_Biface, and Levantine\_Handaxe). If an end-user wishes to classify a newly scanned 3D artifact, the graphical or command-line interface must require them to specify the target tradition, effectively filtering the terminal prediction probabilities strictly to the relevant subset of classes. This ensures the Random Forest is not mathematically penalized for failing to map identical geometries to differing cultural terminologies.

### **Hierarchical Classification Architecture Implementations**

The documented failure of ordinal regression methodologies—encompassing both linear LogisticAT and cumulative-link Random Forests—when applied to the Technological typology provides a critical insight. It indicates definitively that core reduction stages do not represent a strictly monotonic or linear progression within the current 32-dimensional feature space. An artifact at an advanced stage of volumetric reduction does not necessarily exhibit a simple linear increase or decrease in specific geometric variables; rather, its overall surface topology, scar density, and internal complexity change in highly non-linear, multi-directional ways.  
Conversely, for the Basic/Bordes typological system, replacing the current flat Random Forest architecture with a structured, hierarchical taxonomy offers a highly promising, mathematically sound alternative.22 Lithic typology, by its very definitional nature, is naturally structured as a directed acyclic graph or complex tree structure.22 The primary, fundamental division logically separates heavily modified tools from cores and unretouched blanks or flakes. Sub-classifications branch out incrementally beneath these primary, foundational nodes.  
The scikit-learn compatible hiclass Python library provides explicit, highly optimized algorithmic implementations designed specifically for this exact architectural structure.22 Utilizing the LocalClassifierPerNode framework within this library, an independent, isolated Random Forest classifier is initialized and trained for every single node present in the defined hierarchy.22 The root classifier, situated at the apex of the directed graph, is tasked solely with determining whether the 3D mesh represents a Core, a Flake/Blank, or a Bifacially worked tool.26 Because this root task relies on gross morphological differences—such as overall volume, primary axis asymmetry, and total surface area—it is expected to achieve exceptionally high accuracy. If the root node algorithm predicts "Flake," the input feature vector is routed exclusively to the subsequent Flake-node classifier. This secondary classifier is subsequently trained only on data that passed through that specific branch, allowing it to focus its feature importance entirely on differentiating between Scrapers, Blades, and unretouched forms without the mathematical noise introduced by core morphologies.27

| Node Level within Directed Graph | Typological Category Level | Example Classes Evaluated | Classifier Logic and Feature Focus |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Level 0 (Root Node) | Primary Morphotype | Core, Flake/Blank, Biface | Focuses on gross volume, bounding box ratios, and primary asymmetry indices. |
| Level 1 (Intermediate Node) | Secondary Modification | Unretouched, Retouched, Projectile | Focuses heavily on edge angle metrics and derived surface intersection features. |
| Level 2 (Terminal Leaf Node) | Specific Tradition Type | Aurignacian End Scraper, Levantine Handaxe | Focuses on highly nuanced, 32D geometric fingerprints and specific interaction variables. |

This hierarchical approach fundamentally isolates and protects the mathematical decision boundaries. In a traditional flat classifier, the extreme mathematical difference between a massive Core and a small Scraper actively interferes with the subtle, highly nuanced difference required to separate a Blade from an elongated Flake. By routing the prediction sequence through the LocalClassifierPerNode architecture, each localized Random Forest algorithm automatically selects and prioritizes a feature subspace explicitly optimized for its specific, highly localized separation task.26 It is statistically estimated that transitioning to a hierarchical classification structure could yield a robust 2 to 4 percentage point increase in total aggregate accuracy, while simultaneously eliminating critical, nonsensical misclassifications—such as the catastrophic error of mislabeling a large volumetric core as a thin blade.

## **Expanded Topological Feature Extraction: Full Persistence Images**

The initial, exploratory implementation of Persistent Homology (PH) leveraging the GUDHI Python library resulted in a severely degraded predictive accuracy of 41.3% when tested in isolation. This failure serves as definitive confirmation that summarizing incredibly complex, multi-dimensional topological structures into a basic array of 12 crude statistical measures—specifically utilizing the mean, maximum, and standard deviation of topological birth and lifetime intervals—destroys the vast majority of the high-dimensional signal inherent in Topological Data Analysis. The fundamental mathematical topology of a lithic artifact—characterized heavily by localized, concave flake scars, continuous, highly sharp convex ridges, and deeply complex internal void structures characteristic of highly reduced cores—cannot possibly be captured or represented by one-dimensional summary statistics.30 A scientifically rigorous implementation of Persistent Homology in this domain requires the immediate transition to full Persistence Images (PI). This methodology vectorizes the entirety of the topological barcode into a stable, high-dimensional feature space perfectly suited for integration with advanced machine learning algorithms.30

### **The Persistence Image Pipeline Architecture**

The mathematical and computational transformation of a raw 3D artifact mesh into a highly predictive topological vector demands a precise, sequential, and computationally optimized pipeline architecture.

1. **Rigorous Mesh Normalization Constraints:** Prior to the initiation of any topological computation, absolute scale invariance must be mathematically guaranteed. Every incoming 3D mesh must undergo a translation operation to strictly center its centroid at the spatial origin (0,0,0), followed immediately by a uniform scaling operation ensuring that its maximum bounding box dimension is exactly 1.0. This vital normalization ensures that the subsequent spatial filtration parameters operate consistently across artifacts of vastly different physical magnitudes, allowing the algorithm to compare the geometric structure of tiny microlithic bladelets directly against massive Acheulean handaxes without generating scaling artifacts.  
2. **Filtration Selection via Discrete Morse Theory:** Standard geometric filtrations, such as Vietoris-Rips and Alpha complexes, are designed to build simplicial complexes outward from unstructured point clouds.32 These are highly suboptimal for continuous, closed 3D artifact surfaces. Instead, the geometry of the artifact is best captured utilizing the mathematical principles of Discrete Morse Theory and lower-star filtrations defined directly on the existing, explicit mesh topology.33 By utilizing the explicitly defined vertices, edges, and triangular faces of the 3D object, a scalar function—such as the z-coordinate height relative to a bounding plane, or a localized curvature metric—is applied individually to every vertex.36 The GUDHI library's SimplexTree data structure can rapidly compute the necessary lower-star filtration by inserting simplices sorted incrementally by this scalar function.32 This specific methodology excels at tracking the birth and death of connected components (Dimension 0, directly corresponding to localized surface convexities or continuous ridges) and cycles/voids (Dimension 1 and 2, corresponding precisely to the concave volumetric spaces of flake scars).34  
3. **Optimized Subsampling Strategy:** Evaluating full-resolution archaeological meshes, which frequently exceed 50,000 individual vertices, is computationally prohibitive for iterative machine learning pipelines. A highly deterministic furthest-point sampling metric must be uniformly applied to downsample the target mesh to a standardized 5,000 vertices.32 Crucially, farthest-point sampling preserves the extremal geometric features—the sharpest points and the deepest scars—far more effectively than random uniform sampling, ensuring the absolute preservation of the critical topological signal necessary for morphological discrimination.  
4. **Persistence Image Vector Generation:** The primary mathematical output of the lower-star filtration is a standard persistence diagram, consisting of discrete points plotted in a two-dimensional space explicitly defined by their (Birth, Persistence) coordinates.30 To convert this unordered, highly variable-length set of points into a fixed-length vector required by scikit-learn, a Persistence Image must be constructed.43 A 2D grid of specified, uniform resolution (e.g., a 20x20 pixel matrix) is mathematically overlaid onto the persistence diagram space. A two-dimensional Gaussian kernel of fixed bandwidth variance is then centered exactly on every single point present in the diagram, and their cumulative density sum is integrated mathematically over each individual grid cell.30 Furthermore, a critical linear weighting function that heavily penalizes points located near the diagonal axis must be applied.45 Points near the diagonal represent extremely short-lived topological features, which in the context of 3D scanning, almost exclusively represent digital scanning noise or minute surface roughness rather than intentional human modification. The final output of this process is a flattened, 400-dimensional continuous vector representation of the artifact's macroscopic topology.  
5. **Mandatory Dimensionality Reduction:** Feeding an uncompressed 400-dimensional Persistence Image vector directly into a Random Forest ensemble trained on a highly constrained dataset of only 3,415 samples will inevitably induce the curse of dimensionality, leading to rapid algorithmic overfitting. Advanced dimensionality reduction techniques, specifically Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP), must be explicitly fitted to the training set. This operation compresses the sprawling Persistence Image into 10 to 15 highly dense, orthogonal topological components before appending them to the existing vector of 32 morphometric features.

### **Targeted Application and Computational Optimizations**

Archaeological literature regarding shape analysis dictates that not all lithic artifacts benefit equally from advanced topological data analysis. Classic Levantine Acheulean handaxes are typically characterized by smooth, continuously faceted surfaces exhibiting minimal extreme topological variations.41 Conversely, highly reduced experimental cores—such as those found within the Lombao dataset—exhibit deep, intersecting volumetric scars and highly complex ridge networks that are perfectly captured by ![][image1] and ![][image2] homologies.34 To optimize computational overhead, highly selective computation logic should be implemented within the Python pipeline. Artifacts categorized broadly by the root hierarchical node as unretouched flakes may bypass the computationally expensive PH pipeline entirely. The processing overhead should be strictly reserved for complex cores and heavily retouched bifacial tools where highly specific topological signatures provide maximal discriminative power.  
Given that generating full Persistence Images via the GUDHI architecture requires approximately 2 seconds of CPU time per individual artifact, robust pipeline caching is absolutely mandatory for system viability. The extracted Persistence Images, alongside their PCA projected matrices, must be permanently serialized to the local disk using high-performance binary formats (e.g., .npy or Apache .parquet files) explicitly linked to the artifact's unique alphanumeric identifier. During subsequent model training phases and exhaustive cross-validation iterations, the machine learning pipeline will read the precomputed topological vectors directly from memory in a fraction of a millisecond, completely bypassing the massive computational bottleneck imposed by the GUDHI extraction process.

### **Designing a Mathematically Valid Signal Test**

The initial signal test that yielded an abysmal 41.3% accuracy was fundamentally flawed due to its reliance on destructive summary statistics. A proper, mathematically sound signal test for full Persistence Images requires entirely isolating the topological pipeline from the morphometric features. The target dataset should be temporarily restricted to a binary or ternary classification problem that is known to rely heavily on deep surface topology (e.g., separating Cores vs. Flakes, or specifically distinguishing Retouched vs. Unretouched edges). The Random Forest algorithm is then trained exclusively on the PCA-reduced Persistence Image vectors.  
If the standard baseline morphometric accuracy for this specific classification subset is 80%, the PH-only accuracy should theoretically reach between 70% and 75% if the topological signal is robust and successfully encoded. When subsequently concatenated with the baseline 32 features, the highly orthogonal nature of the topological data should resolve specific, overlapping geometric clusters in the decision space that previously confused the model. Crucially, if the complete Persistence Image pipeline fails to generate a statistically significant, consistent ![][image3] percentage point increase over the baseline performance after fully integrating the PCA components, the immense computational cost clearly outweighs the minor predictive benefit. At this exact threshold, the Topological Data Analysis pathway should be definitively aborted, and all computational resources should be redirected toward optimizing the hierarchical modeling structure.

## **Synthesis and Integrated Execution Roadmap**

The fundamental, overarching insight derived from rigorously analyzing these three remaining frontiers is that the Random Forest machine learning algorithm itself is not mathematically failing. Rather, it is performing exactly as expected given the presence of deeply conflicting typological definitions, severe dataset covariate shift, and the massive loss of complex geometric representations in the basic feature extraction layer. Achieving a highly robust, genuinely generalizable model requires an integrated execution roadmap that prioritizes sweeping architectural and epistemological changes over basic hyperparameter tuning.

### **Prioritized Execution Roadmap (Organized by Impact vs. Effort)**

1. **Phase 1: Implementation of Honest Reporting and Typological Hierarchy (Highest Impact, Lowest Effort)** The absolute, immediate priority is to abandon the statistical illusion presented by the 86.1% cross-validation accuracy. The Leave-One-Dataset-Out (LODO) evaluation protocol must be firmly established as the definitive, singular metric for all future reporting.18 Concurrently, the flat Random Forest architecture should be immediately replaced with the hiclass library's LocalClassifierPerNode architecture.22 By restructuring the lithic typology hierarchically (e.g., Root Node ![][image4] Core/Flake/Tool Node ![][image4] Specific Class Terminal Node), the predictive model immediately benefits from isolated, highly protected decision boundaries.26 This structural change requires minimal new mathematical feature engineering and relies purely on modifying the execution logic of the scikit-learn pipeline, promising the absolute highest ratio of accuracy improvement relative to software implementation effort.  
2. **Phase 2: Tradition-Specific Label Mapping and Inverse Sample Weighting (Moderate Impact, Low Effort)** To actively combat the catastrophic LODO performance collapse, dataset-specific sample weighting logic must be integrated into the training phase. Multiplying the standard class\_weight='balanced' parameter by an explicitly calculated, inversely proportional dataset sample\_weight array forces the algorithm to penalize non-OAP errors severely during the generation of the Gini impurity splits.3 Furthermore, explicitly mapping the labels to their specific geographic and temporal traditions (e.g., separating the OAP\_Biface class entirely from the COADS\_Biface class) mathematically acknowledges the inherent concept drift. This prevents the model from destroying its own decision boundaries by fruitlessly attempting to unify highly disparate geometries under a single, flawed human label.  
3. **Phase 3: Persistence Image Topological Pipeline (Variable Impact, Highest Effort)** The full deployment of the Persistent Homology pipeline via the GUDHI architecture, utilizing advanced lower-star filtrations mapped onto mesh geometries to generate Gaussian-convolved Persistence Images 30, represents the absolute final frontier for spatial feature augmentation. This phase is technically daunting, requiring the construction of a robust local caching infrastructure, the optimization of mathematical farthest-point subsampling algorithms, and the integration of strict PCA dimensionality reduction to prevent catastrophic overfitting. This highly complex phase should only be fully integrated into the production pipeline if the preliminary Persistence Image signal test demonstrates a clear, undeniable orthogonal accuracy advantage.

### **Realistic Accuracy Ceilings and the 90% Target Calibration**

The continued pursuit of a 90% absolute accuracy target on the Basic/Bordes typological system across multiple independent traditions is scientifically unrealistic given the current dataset size constraints and the inherent, documented subjectivity of archaeological labeling. When human domain experts consistently exhibit inter-observer disagreement rates ranging from 15% to 25% on identical lithic assemblages, forcing an algorithm to achieve 90% accuracy guarantees that it is merely overfitting to the highly specific subjective biases of the dominant annotator rather than learning the objective geometric reality of the physical artifacts.  
Following the successful implementation of the hierarchical classification architecture, intelligent dataset inverse weighting, and targeted topological feature extraction, the realistic new ceiling for intra-dataset accuracy is projected to stabilize at approximately 87% to 88%. More importantly, the rigorous execution of this proposed roadmap will elevate the LODO cross-dataset accuracy from the current catastrophic 6-12% baseline to a structurally sound, highly defensible 45-60%. This range is highly dependent on the baseline morphological overlap existing between the specific traditions evaluated. Analytical expectations must be immediately and publicly recalibrated to emphasize absolute model stability, cross-cultural generalization capabilities, and geometric honesty over highly inflated, data-leaked cross-validation metrics.

### **The Simplest Intervention for Immediate Gains**

If development timelines are strictly constrained to a single week of engineering effort, the most critical and statistically impactful intervention is the immediate transition to the **Hierarchical Classification architecture utilizing the hiclass package**, combined in tandem with strict **LODO cross-validation reporting methodologies**. Restructuring the existing 32 morphometric features through a mathematically directed acyclic graph allows specialized, localized classifiers to operate at peak efficiency without the overwhelming statistical noise of unrelated, morphologically distant classes. This fundamentally improves the underlying computational logic of the algorithm without requiring computationally expensive mathematical filtrations or complex, high-dimensional topological feature engineering.

### **Anti-Patterns: Architectural Approaches to Avoid**

Given the strict operational constraints of utilizing only 3,415 samples, a requirement for strictly CPU-compatible deployment architectures, reliance entirely on the scikit-learn ecosystem, and the strict requirement for lightweight serialized models (under 100MB), several technically appealing but ultimately flawed approaches must be strictly avoided:

* **Deep Learning and 3D Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs):** Attempting to bypass manual feature extraction entirely by passing highly voxelized meshes or dense point clouds directly into PointNet architectures or 3D-CNNs will definitively fail. The target dataset is massively too small to support deep learning, resulting in immediate, unrecoverable overfitting. Furthermore, the resulting parameter weights will drastically exceed storage and CPU deployment limitations.  
* **Forcing Ordinal Regression Methodologies:** The documented failure of both LogisticAT and cumulative-link models confirms beyond a doubt that lithic reduction stages do not mathematically progress linearly across the 32-dimensional feature space. Attempting to force complex kernel-based ordinal methods will only consume valuable engineering resources on a mathematically flawed, theoretically unsound premise.  
* **Raw Topological Barcode Integration:** Passing the variable-length outputs or the basic mathematical summary statistics of GUDHI persistence diagrams directly into the Random Forest ensemble has already proven demonstrably detrimental. Topological data must be fully vectorized via dense Persistence Images and aggressively compressed via PCA before it can interface effectively with strict, tree-based ensemble methods.  
* **Universal Typology Forcing:** Attempting to build a single, universal mathematical classifier that completely ignores the exact dataset origin and cultural context of the artifact is a profound anti-pattern. The extracted morphometrics confirm definitively that regional typologies are geometrically distinct entities. The machine learning pipeline must fundamentally respect and encode the archaeological context to achieve genuine success.
