# Beyond the Classifier: Unlocking Lithic Typology Accuracy by Reframing Class Ambiguity and Data Bias

## Establishing the Theoretical Performance Ceiling

Before pursuing further enhancements to the classification pipeline, it is imperative to establish a realistic performance ceiling for the given dataset and feature set. This foundational step prevents the misallocation of resources toward an unattainable goal and ensures that subsequent efforts are focused on the most promising avenues. The theoretical ceiling can be understood through two primary lenses: an empirical benchmark derived from human judgment and an information-theoretic limit defined by statistical pattern recognition principles. The first lens involves assessing the reliability of the ground-truth labels themselves, while the second provides a mathematical lower bound on the error rate any classifier can achieve.

An immediate and crucial empirical benchmark is the inter-annotator agreement (IAA) among human lithic experts. It is commonly claimed that IAA represents the absolute performance ceiling for machine learning models on a given task [[4](https://aclanthology.org/2022.bionlp-1.26.pdf)]. While this claim is nuanced—models can sometimes outperform humans if they learn different underlying patterns—it correctly identifies that the ambiguity or inconsistency within the ground-truth labels imposes a fundamental limit on model performance [[4](https://aclanthology.org/2022.bionlp-1.26.pdf)]. In the context of lithic analysis, where typological systems can be subjective, IAA serves as a powerful proxy for the inherent noise in the data. The provided context estimates that human inter-annotator agreement typically falls within the 70–80% range <user>. The current model's accuracy of 84.8% for the Basic and Bordes typologies already exceeds this range. This discrepancy suggests one of two possibilities: either the typology system possesses a degree of objective consistency that the model has captured, or the model is overfitting to idiosyncrasies or errors present in the training labels. Given that the training data originates from five distinct archaeological sources (OAP, Levantine Acheulean, COADS, Lombao Cores, Morales Retouch), each potentially adhering to slightly different interpretive traditions, label ambiguity is a significant concern <user>. For instance, a "Side Scraper" classified in the Open Aurignacian Project (OAP) collection from Italy may not align perfectly with a "Side Scraper" from the COADS collection in Ohio <user>. Therefore, a direct comparison between human expert labeling and model predictions is essential. Commissioning a small-scale study where domain experts re-annotate a subset of the dataset could provide a definitive human baseline, contextualizing whether the current 84.8% accuracy is indeed superior to human performance or if it reflects a more complex relationship with the noisy ground truth.

For a more rigorous, information-theoretic assessment, the Bayes Error Rate (BER) offers a fundamental statistical lower bound on the generalizable error of any classifier for a given problem and set of features [[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.03159v3), [15](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/253933863_Bayes_Error_Rate_Estimation_Using_Classifier_Ensembles)]. The BER represents the probability of misclassification that would result from an optimal decision rule based on the true underlying probability distributions of the classes. Estimating the BER provides a hard, mathematical ceiling on achievable accuracy, independent of the specific algorithm used. If the class distributions for different typological types are highly overlapping in the 22-dimensional feature space, the BER will be high, signaling that no amount of model tuning will yield near-perfect accuracy. Conversely, a low BER would indicate that the pursuit of high accuracy is plausible. Several methodologies exist for estimating the BER, including techniques based on classifier ensembles and bias-corrected estimators suitable for small sample sizes [[15](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/253933863_Bayes_Error_Rate_Estimation_Using_Classifier_Ensembles), [48](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12761056/)]. While computationally intensive, undertaking a BER estimation would provide the most objective measure of the task's feasibility. This analysis would directly address the user's query about whether 90%+ accuracy is theoretically possible. If the estimated BER is, for example, 15%, then the maximum achievable accuracy is 85%, making the 90% goal unrealistic. This information would pivot the project's focus from chasing a specific accuracy metric to improving reliability and providing well-calibrated confidence scores, a valuable outcome even if macro-accuracy plateaus below the target.

Further investigation into the nature of the data itself is required to understand the sources of this performance ceiling. A critical factor is the extreme heterogeneity of the dataset. The Open Aurignacian Project (OAP) collection alone comprises 2,418 of the 3,415 samples, representing approximately 71% of the entire dataset <user>. This severe class imbalance and data-source concentration create a high risk of dataset-specific biases. The model may learn to recognize patterns characteristic of European Upper Palaeolithic artefacts from OAP rather than universal typological categories, leading to poor generalization to artefacts from other traditions like the Levantine Acheulean or COADS projectile points <user>. To mitigate this, the evaluation protocol must evolve beyond simple stratified k-fold cross-validation. A more robust approach would be to employ a grouped or leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation scheme <Expert 1><Expert 5>. In a leave-one-dataset-out scenario, the model would be trained on four collections and tested exclusively on the fifth. Repeating this for each of the five datasets provides a much clearer picture of the model's ability to generalize to novel lithic traditions. If performance drops significantly when testing on a dataset not seen during training (e.g., predicting Levantine Acheulean types from a model trained only on OAP and COADS), it would confirm that the bottleneck lies in the variability between archaeological assemblages, not just within them. This finding would have profound implications, suggesting that the typology system itself may be the primary issue, as its definitions might not be consistently applied across different cultural contexts <user>.

Finally, latent typological structures within the data should be explored. A "typology consistency audit" is a necessary step before optimizing the model <Expert 6>. This involves clustering the feature space without reference to the assigned typology labels to see what natural groupings emerge. If these clusters align well with the existing typology classes, it provides evidence that the labels are meaningful and recoverable from the geometry alone. However, if the clusters reveal a different structure—for example, grouping together artefacts from different typological classes that share similar morphometric properties—it strongly suggests that the current typology is not fully recoverable from the available 22 features [[40](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41982-021-00097-2)]. This could be because the typology relies on attributes not captured by the morphometrics, such as surface attributes (colour, material type) or technological trace evidence like hafting residues [[41](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6586293/)]. Such a finding would shift the research focus entirely away from model optimization and toward identifying and incorporating these missing explanatory variables. In summary, establishing the performance ceiling requires a multi-pronged approach: benchmarking against human expert agreement, estimating the information-theoretic Bayes Error Rate, and critically auditing the consistency and generalizability of the typology labels across the heterogeneous dataset. Only after this foundational work can targeted interventions be justified and prioritized.

## Optimizing Model and Feature Engineering for High-Impact Gains

Once the performance ceiling is established and the primary bottlenecks are identified, the next logical step is to explore model-centric and feature-centric optimizations. These strategies aim to extract the maximum predictive power from the existing dataset and feature set. While it is unlikely that these methods alone can bridge a large gap to a 90% accuracy threshold if the typology system is fundamentally ambiguous, they are essential for maximizing performance and are likely to yield incremental but valuable improvements. The current model, a `RandomForestClassifier` with default parameters, serves as a strong baseline, but it has not been systematically optimized for this specific tabular dataset of ~3,415 samples with imbalanced classes <user>.

Model-centric improvements begin with systematic hyperparameter tuning. The default configuration used in the current setup is a reasonable starting point, but it is almost certainly suboptimal <user>. A more sophisticated search strategy, such as Bayesian optimization, would be far more efficient than a basic grid or random search for finding the best combination of parameters. Key parameters to tune include `max_depth` (to control for overfitting), `min_samples_split` and `min_samples_leaf` (to manage node purity and prevent the model from learning spurious patterns from minority classes), `max_features` (the number of features to consider at each split), and `criterion` (the function to measure the quality of a split, e.g., 'gini' vs. 'entropy') <Expert 1>. Given the relatively small sample size (~3,415), simpler models often perform better than overly complex ones, but the right balance is key. Furthermore, exploring alternative ensemble architectures to Random Forest is highly recommended. Gradient Boosting Machines (GBMs) like LightGBM and CatBoost are frequently reported to outperform Random Forest on tabular data, especially when dealing with imbalanced classes, due to their iterative focus on correcting previous errors <Expert 4>. Their sparse gradient updates can be particularly effective in this context. Another strong candidate is the Support Vector Machine (SVM) with a Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel. SVMs are known for their excellent performance on small-to-medium-sized tabular problems when hyperparameters like `C` (regularization) and `gamma` (kernel coefficient) are carefully tuned <Expert 4>. Comparing the performance of an optimally tuned RF, a GBM, and an SVM would provide a clearer picture of which algorithm is best suited to this specific problem. Finally, stacking or superlearning, an ensemble method where multiple base models (e.g., RF, GBM, SVM) make predictions that are then combined by a meta-learner (like a Logistic Regression model), is a proven technique for boosting accuracy on many tabular datasets [[6](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/236005683_Pattern_Classification_Using_Ensemble_Methods), [8](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-9717/13/11/3653), [18](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396448243_Enhancing_Classification_Accuracy_Using_Stacked_Ensemble_Learning_A_Hybrid_Ensemble)]. This approach can capture complementary strengths from different algorithms, potentially yielding a modest but consistent accuracy increase <Expert 1>.

While model architecture is important, feature engineering can unlock hidden signal within the data. The current set of 22 hand-crafted morphometric features is a solid foundation, but it may not be exhaustive <user>. A first, low-effort step is to generate new features by creating interactions between the existing ones. Simple operations like taking the product, ratio, or difference of pairs of features could uncover non-linear relationships that the model cannot easily learn from axis-aligned splits. For example, while elongation (L/W) and flatness (W/T) are already included, features like L×W or W/T² might provide additional discriminatory power <Expert 3>. Applying polynomial or logarithmic transformations to key features could also help linearize relationships and improve model fit <Expert 3>. Another semi-supervised technique involves running an unsupervised clustering algorithm, such as k-means or DBSCAN, on the feature space. The resulting cluster assignments or distances to cluster centers can then be added as new features for the final classifier, effectively injecting information about the local density and structure of the data into the supervised learning phase <Expert 3>.

For higher-impact but also higher-complexity feature engineering, the application of advanced geometric and topological descriptors is a promising avenue. These methods aim to capture global shape characteristics that may be missed by local measurements. Persistent Homology, a tool from Topological Data Analysis (TDA), computes the topological features (such as connected components, loops, and voids) of a shape at multiple scales [[14](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/engineering/bayes-error)]. These features are remarkably robust to noise and non-rigid deformations, making them highly suitable for archaeological artefacts which may be fragmented or worn. By computing persistence diagrams or barcodes from the mesh vertex cloud or a filtration of the mesh, one can derive a set of summary statistics (e.g., total persistence, number of significant features) that serve as powerful, rotation-invariant global shape descriptors <Expert 3>. Libraries like GUDHI are specifically designed for this purpose <Expert 3>. Other advanced geometric descriptors include Spherical Harmonics (SH), which fit a spherical harmonic decomposition to the mesh surface to produce a set of coefficients that act as global shape descriptors; Heat Kernel Signatures (HKS), which describe shape at multiple scales; and 3D Zernike Moments, which are rotation-invariant moments calculated from a voxelized representation of the mesh <Expert 3>. These techniques have shown success in academic literature for 3D shape retrieval and classification [[37](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0960982217312459), [47](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-017-03867-7)]. However, they come with significant implementation challenges, including the need for clean, watertight meshes and substantial computational overhead. A pragmatic approach would be to first implement the simpler feature interactions, as this is computationally inexpensive and easy to integrate. If these show promise, the team can then evaluate the feasibility of implementing Persistent Homology, as it appears to be a more general-purpose and robust technique for archaeological contexts compared to SH or Zernike moments, which may have stricter geometric requirements (e.g., star-shaped meshes for SH). Ultimately, the path to improvement lies in a systematic exploration of both model configurations and the feature space itself, moving from low-effort, high-impact changes to more complex, exploratory techniques.

## Reframing the Typology System as the Primary Bottleneck

The most compelling evidence from the provided context suggests that the primary obstacle to achieving 90%+ accuracy is not the choice of machine learning algorithm or the sophistication of the features, but rather the typology system itself. The very definition of the "ground truth" appears to be a significant source of ambiguity and inconsistency, limiting the performance ceiling regardless of how well the model is optimized. The willingness to modify the target variable is therefore not just an option but a strategic necessity <user>. Addressing the inherent flaws or limitations within the typological framework is poised to deliver the largest and most impactful gains in classification accuracy.

A critical issue undermining the integrity of the dataset is its extreme heterogeneity and severe class imbalance. The Open Aurignacian Project (OAP) collection, comprising 2,418 samples, constitutes over 70% of the entire training set <user>. This massive preponderance of a single source introduces a profound risk of dataset-specific bias. The classifier may not be learning the universal geometric signatures of a "Biface" or a "Flake," but rather the specific stylistic or manufacturing conventions of the Aurignacian tradition represented in the OAP. When evaluated on data from other traditions, such as the Levantine Acheulean or the projectile-point-heavy COADS collection, its performance is likely to degrade significantly <user>. This phenomenon highlights a fundamental weakness in the assumption that typological labels are universally consistent across different archaeological cultures and time periods. A "Biface" from Fumane Cave in Italy may have morphological characteristics that differ subtly but meaningfully from a "Biface" from a site in Israel, yet the typology treats them as interchangeable classes [[32](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0305440323001693)]. This inconsistency between annotators or source datasets would inherently limit accuracy, regardless of model quality <user>. To diagnose this, a "Typology Consistency Audit" is paramount <Expert 6>. This involves comparing how different source collections classify artefacts that appear to belong to the same broad morphological type. For example, one could analyze the distribution of morphometric features for all artefacts labeled "Scraper" across the OAP, Levantine Acheulean, and COADS collections. If the feature distributions are significantly different, it suggests that the label "Scraper" corresponds to different underlying shapes in different contexts, making it a weak discriminant. This audit would provide invaluable context for interpreting model performance and guide decisions on whether to merge classes or redefine typological boundaries.

Further evidence for typological ambiguity comes from the initial intervention that yielded a +3.2 percentage point accuracy boost: the merging of a 4-sample "Tool" class into the larger "Retouched Flake" class <user>. This dramatic improvement from a single action is a powerful signal. It implies that the original "Tool" class was either morphologically indistinct from "Retouched Flake" (suggesting they should be merged) or that the class was an unreliable predictor due to its small size and potential noise. Confusion matrix analysis of the current model is the logical next step to identify other frequently confused class pairs <Expert 6>. For instance, if the matrix consistently shows high confusion between "Scraper" and "Notch/Denticulate," it suggests these classes may represent overlapping points in the morphospace and could benefit from being merged into a broader category like "Edged Artefact." This strategy of simplifying the typology by merging confusable classes is a direct way to reduce the intrinsic ambiguity of the classification task. It shifts the goal from distinguishing between nearly identical objects to distinguishing between more clearly separated morphological groups, which is a more tractable problem for any machine learning model. This approach is advocated by some researchers who propose replacing rigid typologies with methods based on attribute combinations that better reflect behavioral variation [[11](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/251122402_Nineteenth_Century_Tools_for_Twenty-First_Century_Archaeology_Why_the_Middle_Paleolithic_Typology_of_Francois_Bordes_Must_Be_Replaced)].

The case of the Technological typology presents another clear example of a typology system that may be poorly matched to the chosen classification approach. This typology is described as "core reduction stages" (e.g., Early Core, Middle Core, Late Core, Exhausted) <user>. This description reveals its inherently sequential and ordinal nature. Treating this as a standard multi-class classification problem, where the model is penalized equally for any misclassification (e.g., predicting "Middle Core" when the true label is "Late Core" versus predicting "Flake"), is statistically inefficient. A prediction of "Late Core" for an actual "Middle Core" artefact is, in a sense, a "partial success" that the model is not rewarded for. Applying an ordinal regression framework is a highly promising strategy to address this mismatch <Expert 6>. Ordinal regression explicitly models the ordered nature of the classes. Methods exist to convert any standard classifier (like a Random Forest) into an ordinal one. For example, a cumulative link model trains a series of binary classifiers to determine the probability that an artefact belongs to a class above or below a certain threshold (e.g., P(class > Early Core), P(class > Middle Core)) [[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.17278v1)]. Research has shown that explicitly modeling ordinality can lead to significant performance gains, particularly in scenarios with sparse data or many classes, which aligns well with the current dataset [[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.17278v1)]. Using a model-agnostic Python package compatible with scikit-learn, this transformation can be implemented with relatively low effort. This change is not merely a technical tweak; it fundamentally reframes the problem to better match the underlying reality of the data, which is a far more powerful intervention than simply trying a different algorithm. In conclusion, the evidence strongly indicates that the typology system is a major bottleneck. A proactive strategy involving a consistency audit, merging of confusable classes, and the adoption of ordinal regression for the Technological typology represents the most direct and impactful path toward achieving the project's goals.

## Evaluating Advanced Methodologies Within Deployment Constraints

While optimizing models and refining typologies are the most direct paths to improved accuracy, a comprehensive analysis requires evaluating more advanced research methodologies. The user has granted full freedom for research and development, allowing for the use of tools like PyTorch and GUDHI, with the explicit requirement that any final production-ready solution must be deployable via lightweight, CPU-only scikit-learn or ONNX models <user>. This dual constraint necessitates a careful evaluation of each advanced technique: its potential for performance gain versus its implementation complexity and its compatibility with the final deployment pipeline.

Among the most discussed advanced techniques is data augmentation on the 3D meshes themselves. Since acquiring new physical scans is impossible, the idea is to create synthetic variations of the existing ones <Expert 2>. Mesh perturbation, involving small-scale random displacements of vertices, smoothing, or controlled decimation, could generate new training samples that preserve the core typological identity of an artefact. If successful, this could help alleviate the severe class imbalance by artificially inflating the size of minority classes, thereby reducing the risk of overfitting. Another relevant technique is geometric morphometric interpolation, which is particularly pertinent for the Technological typology. By interpolating between two artefacts from adjacent reduction stages, one could generate plausible "in-between" examples, helping the model learn the continuous nature of core reduction <Expert 2>. Similarly, simulating partial occlusion by masking regions of the mesh could improve the model's robustness to damage and incomplete preservation. However, these methods face a significant technical hurdle: every synthetic mesh must pass rigorous validation checks (e.g., being watertight, having non-degenerate faces) to ensure that the downstream morphometric feature extraction pipeline can process it successfully <Expert 2>. The implementation of such a robust mesh manipulation and validation infrastructure is non-trivial and represents a considerable development effort.

Another powerful avenue is self-supervised and semi-supervised learning, which aims to leverage the full dataset, including its inherent structure, to improve learning. Self-training, or pseudo-labelling, involves training an initial model, using it to predict labels for the entire dataset, and then adding the high-confidence predictions back to the training set for a second iteration of training <Expert 5>. This creates a "virtuous cycle" where the model helps itself become more confident and accurate. A more sophisticated approach is contrastive pre-training. Here, all 3,415 samples are used in an unsupervised manner to train a model (e.g., using a SimCLR-style objective) to learn a feature embedding where morphologically similar artefacts are close together in the vector space <Expert 5>. After this pre-training phase, a simple linear classifier can be fine-tuned on top of the frozen embedding network. This approach can capture complex latent structures in the data that standard supervised learning might miss. However, both of these methods require more complex code infrastructure (potentially involving PyTorch) and carry a risk of propagating errors if the initial model is flawed. The key question is whether the benefits of leveraging the entire dataset outweigh the implementation complexity and risk of introducing bias. Given the CPU-compatible deployment constraint, a model trained with PyTorch would need to be carefully exported to an ONNX format that preserves the embedding logic, which may not always be straightforward.

The use of deep learning on raw 3D meshes, such as 3D Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) or PointNet++, must be approached with extreme caution. Despite the research-phase flexibility, a "do not try" recommendation applies to developing a new production pipeline based on these architectures for this specific project. The primary reason is the extremely small dataset size of ~3,415 samples <user>. Deep learning models, particularly those designed for 3D data, possess millions of parameters and have a very high capacity to memorize the training data. With such a limited number of examples, a 3D CNN or PointNet++ would almost certainly suffer from catastrophic overfitting, achieving perfect accuracy on the training set but performing no better than random chance on any unseen data [[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41524-023-01000-z)]. There is insufficient data to properly train such a complex model. Furthermore, these approaches directly violate the project's constraints. Training these models is heavily reliant on GPU acceleration, and deploying the resulting large, complex models may not be feasible within a CPU-only environment, contradicting the lightweight ONNX requirement. While transfer learning from massive public shape repositories like ShapeNet is a possibility, its efficacy on such a small, specialized archaeological dataset is highly uncertain and represents a significant research risk. Therefore, while it is acceptable to *explore* these ideas during the research phase—for instance, to extract features from a pre-trained network—the final deliverable must remain a lightweight, CPU-compatible model from the scikit-learn ecosystem.

Finally, the implementation of advanced geometric and topological features, such as Persistent Homology (using GUDHI), Spherical Harmonics, or Heat Kernel Signatures, falls into a middle ground <Expert 3>. These methods can be computed offline on the existing dataset and the resulting numerical features can be appended to the original feature set. The final classification model would then operate on this enriched feature table, remaining a standard scikit-learn estimator (like a Random Forest or SVM) that can be easily exported to ONNX. This hybrid approach allows for the use of cutting-edge techniques during the feature engineering phase without altering the production deployment pipeline. The main challenge here is the implementation complexity and computational cost of calculating these features, which requires expertise in computational topology or geometry. Persistent Homology, in particular, seems promising due to its robustness to noise and its ability to capture global shape properties that are likely relevant to lithic classification. The trade-off is clear: advanced techniques offer the potential for significant performance leaps but often introduce complexity and risk. A pragmatic strategy would involve prioritizing methods that can be integrated into the existing pipeline (like PH features or ordinal regression) while treating others (like raw-mesh deep learning) as high-risk endeavors to be avoided in the production context.

## A Prioritized Roadmap for Achieving Higher Classification Accuracy

Based on a synthesis of the analysis of the performance ceiling, model optimization, typological bottlenecks, and advanced methodologies, a prioritized roadmap emerges. This roadmap is structured to maximize impact relative to implementation effort, focusing first on addressing the most significant sources of error—the typology system itself—and then proceeding to more complex model and feature refinements. The ultimate goal is to provide a clear, actionable plan to push the Basic/Bordes typology toward 90% and the Technological typology toward 80%, while remaining grounded in the realities of the dataset and deployment constraints.

The highest priority actions are those that address the most fundamental issues with the classification task: the ambiguity of the target variable and the heterogeneity of the data. These are "quick wins" that can be implemented with moderate effort but hold the potential for the highest impact.
First, a **Typology Consistency Audit** and **Confusion Analysis** should be conducted immediately <Expert 6>. This involves analyzing the current model's confusion matrices to identify the most frequently confused class pairs (e.g., Scrapers vs. Notches) and investigating label consistency across the five source datasets (OAP, Levantine Acheulean, etc.). Merging these consistently confused classes, as demonstrated by the prior success of merging the "Tool" class, is a low-effort, high-impact intervention that directly tackles typological ambiguity <user>. Second, the **Technological typology must be reframed as an ordinal regression problem** <Expert 6>. This is arguably the most promising path to improving the weakest score. By explicitly modeling the sequential nature of core reduction stages, this approach can yield significant accuracy gains, especially with sparse data, and is implementable with scikit-learn-compatible libraries [[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.17278v1)]. Third, a **human-expert baseline (Inter-Annotator Agreement) benchmark** should be established <user>. This provides a crucial external reference point to determine if the current 84.8% accuracy is genuinely impressive or if it reflects overfitting to noisy labels. Finally, the evaluation protocol must be updated to use a **leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation scheme** to accurately assess the model's ability to generalize to novel archaeological traditions, mitigating the bias introduced by the OAP-dominated dataset <user>.

The following tier of actions focuses on optimizing the machine learning pipeline once the typological foundations have been addressed. These steps are designed to squeeze out the last few percentage points of performance.
First, a **systematic hyperparameter optimization** of the best-performing model architecture (which could be an optimally tuned Random Forest, a Gradient Boosting Machine like LightGBM, or an SVM) should be performed using a method like Bayesian optimization <Expert 1><Expert 4>. This ensures the chosen model is operating at its peak potential. Second, **simple but effective feature engineering**, such as generating interaction terms (products, ratios) between the existing 22 features, should be undertaken <Expert 3>. This is computationally cheap and can reveal hidden non-linear relationships in the data. Third, if these steps yield positive results, the team should evaluate the implementation of **Persistent Homology (PH)** using the GUDHI library <Expert 3>. PH offers a powerful, robust way to extract global shape information and represents a significant leap in feature sophistication that is still compatible with the final scikit-learn/ONNX deployment pipeline.

The final tier consists of long-term research projects that carry higher risk and complexity but could unlock transformative gains if successful. These should be pursued only after the higher-priority actions have been completed and have plateaued. This includes exploring **advanced geometric feature extraction** methods like Spherical Harmonics or Heat Kernel Signatures, which require deeper expertise and have stricter mesh quality requirements <Expert 3>. It also includes investigating **contrastive self-supervised learning** pre-training, which is computationally intensive and whose benefits on such a small dataset are uncertain, but which could potentially learn a superior feature embedding from the full dataset <Expert 5>.

In direct response to the user's success criteria:
1.  **Can we reach 90%+ on Basic/Bordes?** It is **unlikely** that the typology system itself contains sufficient inherent geometric distinction to support a 90%+ accuracy ceiling, given the evidence of label ambiguity and source heterogeneity. A more realistic goal is to push the accuracy toward a new plateau around 87-89%, assuming the typology inconsistencies (confused classes, source biases) are mitigated through the recommended actions.
2.  **Can we push Technological from 73.6% to 80%?** Yes, this is the most promising target. By reframing the problem as **ordinal regression**, a gain of several percentage points is plausible and represents a direct correction of a methodological mismatch between the data and the classification approach.
3.  **What is the hard ceiling?** The hard ceiling is determined by two factors: the **Bayes Error Rate** (the information-theoretic limit of the feature set) and the **Inter-Annotator Agreement** (the empirical limit of label reliability). These must be quantified through formal estimation and a human benchmarking study, respectively.
4.  **Should we try deep learning on raw meshes?** **No, not for a production-ready pipeline.** The dataset is too small, posing an extreme risk of overfitting, and it violates the CPU-compatible deployment constraint.
5.  **Is the typology system the bottleneck?** **Yes, the evidence strongly supports this.** The extreme data imbalance, the proven success of merging classes, and the ordinal nature of the Technological typology all point to the ground-truth labels and their definitions as the primary limiting factor.
