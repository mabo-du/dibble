# **Comprehensive Analysis of 3D Lithic Mesh Repositories for Morphometric Classifier Optimization**

The digitization of the archaeological record has fundamentally transformed the analytical paradigms applied to material culture, transitioning the discipline from two-dimensional, caliper-based linear morphometrics to highly dimensional, computationally rigorous three-dimensional spatial analyses.1 The development of machine learning architectures capable of automating the classification of lithic artifacts relies entirely on the availability, fidelity, and typological diversity of the underlying training data.3 For computational platforms designed to extract complex morphometric features—such as continuous edge angles, volumetric asymmetry, and scar topology—the training corpus must reflect the immense geographic, chronological, and techno-typological variation inherent in hominin stone tool production.4  
The current baseline dataset powering the classification engine provides a robust foundation of 2,016 high-resolution meshes encompassing the Italian Upper Palaeolithic sequences of Grotta di Fumane, Grotta di Castelcivita, Grotta della Cala, and Riparo Bombrini.6 While this assemblage offers exceptional metadata quality, relying exclusively on Aurignacian and Gravettian laminar technologies introduces an inherent classification bias. A classifier trained solely on blade and bladelet-dominated assemblages will inevitably struggle to accurately parameterize the morphological variance of bifacial handaxes, highly reduced experimental cores, or late Holocene projectile points. To elevate the morphometric classifier’s cross-validation accuracy beyond its current threshold of 82% across five classes, it is imperative to integrate datasets that introduce novel geometric extremes, spanning the Lower Palaeolithic through the Late Prehistoric periods.  
This report systematically details the availability of over 5,000 additional, downloadable 3D lithic meshes hosted across various open-access scientific repositories, including Zenodo, the Open Science Framework (OSF), Dataverse, and Figshare. The identified datasets have been critically evaluated based on their volumetric integrity, format interoperability (PLY, OBJ, STL, GLB, WRL), metadata depth, and overall utility for machine learning applications. By synthesizing macroscopic regional assemblages with highly controlled experimental reduction sequences, the classifier can be mathematically optimized to recognize underlying physical laws of stone fracture, rather than merely memorizing culturally specific typological boundaries.

## **Morphometric Feature Engineering and the Need for Algorithmic Generalization**

The analytical platform under development is engineered to extract 22 distinct morphometric features—including length, width, thickness, elongation indices, and edge angles—from oriented 3D meshes, assigning them to discrete archaeological typologies (Class: Core/Tool, Blank: Blade/Bladelet/Flake, Technology). The existing 82% cross-validation accuracy indicates that the current model is highly proficient at distinguishing within the morphological variance of the Open Aurignacian Project's dataset.6 However, the geometric parameters of a Protoaurignacian bladelet core differ fundamentally from those of a Lower Palaeolithic Acheulean handaxe or a Paleoindian fluted point.7  
If a neural network or Random Forest algorithm is exposed exclusively to unidirectional or bidirectional laminar cores, its internal feature weights will over-optimize for parallel scar topologies and high length-to-width ratios.3 When presented with a radially flaked Levallois core or a bifacially thinned projectile point, the classifier will likely fail because it has never encountered the mathematical signatures of radial convexity or bilateral symmetry.5  
Therefore, acquiring 5,000+ additional meshes is not merely an exercise in data scaling; it is a prerequisite for feature generalization. Integrating robust macro-assemblages and highly specialized experimental datasets ensures that the classifier's feature extraction algorithms learn to dynamically compute variables across an unbroken continuum of prehistoric technological design.5 The datasets identified in this analysis introduce critical new geometric vectors, including acute edge angle steepening via retouch, original mass estimation through controlled volumetric decay, and complex topological mapping of scar networks.

## **Tier 1: Macro-Assemblages for High-Volume Classifier Training**

To achieve the critical mass required to train robust, high-dimensional machine learning architectures, large-scale macro-assemblages must be prioritized.3 These datasets provide the statistical weight necessary to smooth out localized scanning anomalies and individual knapper idiosyncrasies, offering hundreds of meshes per repository to rapidly expand the training corpus.

### **The Central Ohio Archaeological Digitization Survey (COADS)**

The Central Ohio Archaeological Digitization Survey (COADS) represents a monumental collaborative effort between academic institutions (Ball State University and the University of Akron) and responsible private stewards of archaeological material.15 Designed to leverage massive privately held datasets for regional land-use and morphometric analysis, the project recorded over 12,000 diagnostic points and 5,000 additional stone tools.15 A substantial sample of these artifacts was subjected to high-resolution 3D scanning, resulting in a publicly accessible digital archive intended for landmark-based geometric morphometric (LGM) analysis.15  
Currently, a massive sub-sample of these 3D models (exceeding 600 specific entities) is available for direct download via the Zenodo repository network, primarily under the creator tag "bsu\_aal" (Applied Anthropology Laboratories, Ball State University), as well as through associated Sketchfab collections where the API allows for direct mesh acquisition.18 The meshes are provided primarily in PLY and GLB formats, featuring exceptionally detailed metadata that includes regional typological determinations, raw material sourcing, and exact geographic provenance.19  
The integration of the COADS dataset into a morphometric classifier introduces a profound expansion of the algorithm's geometric parameters. Projectile points, particularly notched, stemmed, and fluted varieties, possess acute edge angles, complex basal thinning scars, and bilateral symmetry profiles that are entirely absent in Aurignacian laminar assemblages.23 By exposing the classifier to these highly stylized Holocene and Late Pleistocene bifacial forms, the platform's capacity to measure elongation, thickness-to-width ratios, and volumetric symmetry will be rigorously stress-tested. The primary analytical challenge with this dataset lies in its crowd-sourced nature; the artifacts represent surface collections and disconnected contexts, meaning that while the geometric data is pristine, the chronological labels rely on typological cross-dating rather than absolute stratigraphy.17

### **Later Acheulean Southern Levantine Handaxes**

A critical repository for deep-time morphological variance is the comprehensive dataset published by Antoine Muller and Leore Grosman, encompassing later Acheulean handaxes from the southern Levant.11 This dataset contains 3D models of handaxes recovered from prominent Lower Palaeolithic sites, including Ma'ayan Barukh, Nahal Zihor, Revadim, and Jaljulia.10 The repository aggregates 533 meshes from recent analyses and incorporates an additional 153 meshes from prior studies, resulting in an exceptional corpus of 686 Acheulean bifaces.10  
The data is hosted on Zenodo (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.14534846) and is distributed in WRL (Virtual Reality Modeling Language) and VRML formats.11 Accompanying the meshes are advanced MATLAB scripts specifically engineered for computing 3D sinuosity, bifacial asymmetry, and continuous edge angle measurements (CEAM).11  
From a machine learning perspective, Acheulean handaxes provide the ultimate test case for algorithms designed to quantify continuous curves and gross volumetric symmetry. Handaxe morphology is dictated by the imposition of hierarchical sub-goals and deliberate edge refinement aimed at maintaining a specific functional equilibrium.25 Incorporating this dataset will require standardizing the WRL formats into PLY or OBJ, a routine geometric transformation. Once converted, the sheer mass and deliberate bilateral symmetry of these artifacts will teach the classifier to differentiate between the opportunistic volumetric extraction seen in simple flake cores and the highly structured, convergent shaping of bifacial tools.24 Furthermore, the continuous edge angle properties of these handaxes—which often feature distinct functional zones of sharpness and blunt prehension areas—will vastly improve the platform's edge-detection algorithms.24

### **The Nubian Levallois Morphometric Database**

Levallois technology represents a monumental cognitive shift in human evolution, characterized by the predetermination of flake morphology through the careful architectural preparation of the core's convexities.5 The "Quantifying Levallois" database, compiled by Emily Hallinan and João Cascalheira, provides a targeted 3D geometric morphometric approach to understanding a specific variant of this technology: the Nubian Levallois core.13  
Hosted on the Open Science Framework (OSF) platform (DOI: 10.17605/OSF.IO/SJ8ZV), the repository contains high-resolution PLY models acquired using an Einscan Pro 2X Plus structured light scanner, alongside supplementary raw data and spatial analytics.27 The overarching research initiative analyzes material from diverse geographies, including the Nile Valley, Dhofar (Oman), and Tweefontein (South Africa), isolating the geometric nuances that separate discrete Nubian morphologies from continuous Levallois reduction strategies.13  
The inclusion of Levallois cores is a mathematical necessity for a robust lithic classifier. Levallois cores possess distinct hierarchical surface structures—an intersection of two asymmetrical convexities—that are highly standardized.29 By training the classifier on Nubian Levallois meshes, the algorithm will learn to detect the central distal ridge and the highly specific platform preparation characteristics that define the technology. Furthermore, the dataset’s focus on the "Levallois spectrum"—viewing core morphology as a fluid continuum of reduction rather than a static typological bin—aligns perfectly with the goals of advanced machine learning, allowing the classifier to output probabilistic continuum scores rather than rigid binary classifications.5

## **Tier 2: Experimental and Specialized Morphometric Baselines**

While massive assemblages provide the volume necessary for deep learning, experimental and specialized datasets provide the controlled "ground truth" variables required to calibrate highly specific morphometric features, such as reduction intensity, original mass estimation, and edge modification. Because archaeological specimens are recovered only in their final discarded state, the classifier cannot natively deduce how much volume was lost during knapping. Experimental archaeology provides the missing sequential variables.

### **Experimental Core Reduction Trajectories (Lombao)**

Understanding the reduction intensity of a stone tool—the degree to which it has been exhausted from its original raw material volume—is notoriously difficult to quantify using purely archaeological specimens, as the original mass is permanently lost.30 To mathematically parameterize this loss, Diego Lombao and colleagues published an invaluable dataset titled "3D models experimental cores" on Zenodo (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.2585423).32  
This repository contains 290 high-resolution PLY meshes detailing the sequential reduction of quartzite river cobbles.32 The dataset features 30 baseline cobbles (capturing the original raw mass) and 260 subsequent 3D models representing cores at ten different stages of controlled reduction.32 The experimental knapping was executed using distinct technological strategies, including unifacial unipolar, bifacial multipolar centripetal, and multifacial multipolar reduction.33  
For a morphometric classifier, this dataset acts as a localized Rosetta Stone for volumetric decay. Because the exact starting volume and the precise sequence of flake removals are known, the classifier can be trained to regress the current core volume against its estimated original mass. Algorithms like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (CGANs) can ingest these sequential 3D meshes to mathematically map how specific scar topologies correlate with overall mass loss, creating a predictive index for reduction intensity that can subsequently be applied to unknown archaeological material.4 By utilizing this dataset, the platform's 22 morphometric features will be enriched with a dynamic temporal dimension, enabling the classifier to recognize a heavily exhausted core not merely as a small tool, but as the final stage of a prolonged reduction continuum.

### **Retouch Intensity and Flake Modification (Morales)**

Parallel to the volumetric reduction of cores is the morphological modification of the flakes themselves. Juan Ignacio Morales et al. published a foundational dataset addressing this specific variable: "Measuring Retouch Intensity in Lithic Tools: A New Proposal Using 3D Scan Data" (Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.1405047).35  
This extensive 2.5 GB repository contains highly detailed STL files of over 30 experimental flakes, captured both in their pristine, unmodified state and after successive stages of controlled retouching (labeled sequentially as X and Xb).35 The artifacts were produced from tertiary evaporitic flint via freehand hard hammer percussion.35  
By providing the exact before-and-after spatial coordinates of retouched flakes, this dataset allows a machine learning model to explicitly isolate the geometric signature of retouching.36 Training the platform on these paired meshes will enable the classifier to automatically differentiate between the natural feathered termination of a pristine flake margin and the abrupt, invasive, or stepped topography of a heavily resharpened scraper. This capability is paramount, as the platform's stated features include edge angle analysis. Training on the Morales dataset will allow the software to map the steepening trajectory of working edges as they are progressively exhausted, directly correlating edge angle metrics with the "Tool" class in the typological ontology.36 Furthermore, recent advancements have already demonstrated that Random Forest models trained on this type of data can predict the original flake mass with an R-squared value of 0.97, proving the immense computational utility of these paired experimental meshes.3

## **Tier 3: Paleoindian, Caddo, and Deep-Time Regional Repositories**

To ensure the classifier is not geographically biased toward Eurasian or African assemblages, the integration of North American and distinct peripheral datasets is essential. These repositories often feature unique flaking technologies and raw material constraints that force the algorithm to broaden its feature recognition capabilities.

### **Paleoindian and Caddo Morphometrics (Selden)**

Robert Z. Selden Jr. and colleagues have systematically digitized numerous pivotal assemblages that capture the lithic variability of the American continent. Hosted across Zenodo, the Open Science Framework, and the Stephen F. Austin State University ScholarWorks network, these datasets feature exceptionally detailed 3D models of projectile points and bifaces.  
The collections are divided into several key groups. First, 3D scans of pristine Clovis-era artifacts from the Blackwater Draw National Historic Landmark (LA3324) are available in STL and PLY formats.37 These models capture the complex fluting technology indicative of the Paleoindian period, characterized by massive basal thinning scars and distinct concave morphologies.23 Second, a companion dataset features Clovis-age artifacts from the highly stratified workshop at the Gault site in Texas.39 The assemblage includes finely crafted blades, flakes, and bifaces that document the operational sequence of Clovis point manufacture. Finally, a specialized repository (Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.3457943) contains meshes of Caddo Gahagan bifaces from the Mounds Plantation and George C. Davis sites.40  
The inclusion of fluted projectile points and specialized ceremonial bifaces forces the morphometric classifier to adapt to entirely distinct technological rulesets. The geometric mechanics of fluting—where a single channel flake is driven longitudinally from the proximal base—create a unique negative concavity that the algorithm must learn to distinguish from standard bifacial thinning or Levallois preparation. Furthermore, Selden's work heavily utilizes 3D coordinate superimposition and Procrustes analysis, mathematical techniques that the classification platform must internalize to correctly orient and compare morphologically disparate objects.41

### **Biśnik Cave and Figshare Disparate Collections**

To further diversify the Middle Palaeolithic training data, the Biśnik Cave dataset published by Adam Kobyłka and colleagues (Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19186143) offers 3D models of late Middle Pleistocene artifacts from Central Europe.42 The assemblage features local flint artifacts associated with Levallois and other non-bifacial methods. While smaller in scale, this dataset introduces regional material constraints and technological variations from beyond the Movius line, preventing the classifier from over-fitting to Levantine or African raw material signatures.42  
Simultaneously, a systematic query of the Figshare data repository reveals several highly specific datasets published alongside morphometric studies. Notable inclusions are a Middle Paleolithic basalt handaxe from Lastarri (Basque Country, DOI: 10.6084/m9.figshare.17026085) 43, late Oldowan subspheroids from Barranco León (Spain) 44, Middle Stone Age prepared core technology meshes 45, and 3D statistical models of Holocene unifacial tools from Central Brazil.46 While these datasets are somewhat fragmented, aggregating them provides the classifier with exposure to rare typologies—such as subspheroids and basalt bifaces—that exhibit significantly different raw material fracture mechanics than fine-grained cherts.  
*Note on Biological Repositories:* A review of MorphoSource indicates that while it is primarily utilized for biological anthropology and palaeontology (e.g., baboon skulls and hominin long bones), it does host a limited number of lithic artifacts, including 3D meshes of El Jobo tradition projectile point fragments.48 However, the extraction overhead and relative sparsity of purely lithic datasets make MorphoSource a lower priority compared to Zenodo and OSF.

## **Tier 4: Advanced Topologies, Refits, and Scar Network Segmentation**

Advanced morphometric classification goes beyond merely sorting complete artifacts into basic typological bins; the future of computational lithic analysis lies in automating the reconstruction of operational sequences (chaîne opératoire) and extracting micro-topological features like individual flake scars.51 Tier 4 datasets are specifically designed to train these highly complex analytical functions.

### **Topology-Based Scar Detection (Linsel et al.)**

Florian Linsel, Hubert Mara, and colleagues have produced groundbreaking datasets focused on the algorithmic segmentation of 3D meshes into discrete scar networks. Their repository, "Linking Scars: Topology-based Scar Detection and Graph Modeling of Paleolithic Artifacts in 3D" (Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.10477448), provides manually annotated ground-truth data derived from Upper Palaeolithic tools from Grotta di Fumane and experimental knapping series.52  
This dataset fundamentally moves beyond the gross morphology of the artifact and maps the underlying mathematical topology using Multi-Scale Integral Invariants (MSII) curvature values and Morse theory-based algorithms.51 The models represent artifacts as directed adjacency graphs, where nodes signify individual flake scars and edges signify the chronological sequence of removals.51  
For a platform extracting 22 morphometric features, integrating graph-based scar topology is revolutionary. Rather than treating a 3D mesh as a single unified object, the classifier can be trained to recognize the artifact as an aggregate of sequential geometric events. By training on Linsel’s annotated ground-truth models, the classifier can learn to automatically predict the relative temporal order of adjacent scars, objectively quantifying the artifact's operational sequence and removing the subjective interpretation bias inherent in traditional diacritic diagrams.51 This allows the "Technology" label in the metadata to be derived mathematically rather than visually.

### **Virtual Reconstruction of Lithic Reduction (ReViBE)**

The ReViBE (Refit Visualisation using Blender Engine) protocol, published by Javier Sánchez-Martínez and Katia Calmet, offers an unprecedented look into the spatial and temporal dynamics of stone fracture.54 Housed in the CORA Dataverse repository (DOI: 10.34810/data924), this dataset provides high-definition 3D models (.obj and.mtl formats) of a complete refitting sequence (Set 41\) from the 497D level of Cova Gran de Santa Linya.55  
The dataset contains nine individual lithic pieces that seamlessly reconstruct a singular core volume.56 While traditional 3D scanning captures an artifact at its moment of discard, refit sequences capture the negative space and intersecting topologies of multiple interconnected artifacts. Training the classifier on 3D refit sets allows the underlying neural network to understand spatial interlocking and negative volumetric voids. This capability is paramount for future iterations of the platform that may seek to automate the refitting of massive, disjointed lithic scatters by mathematically matching complementary topological surfaces.55

## **Methodological Integration and Classifier Calibration Strategy**

The transition from a regionally confined, period-specific dataset (the 2,016 meshes of the Open Aurignacian Project) to a global, diachronic, and technologically diverse training corpus carries profound computational implications for machine learning architecture.58 To fully leverage the \>5,000 meshes identified in this report, the analytical platform must implement robust data harmonization protocols.

### **Overcoming the Orientation and Alignment Hurdle**

A persistent constraint in automated lithic analysis is the necessity for consistent orientation.32 Traditional geometric morphometrics relies heavily on homologous landmarks—discrete anatomical points that correspond exactly across all specimens.1 However, stone tools lack biological homology; a flake does not possess the inherent biological symmetry of a cranium.2  
Datasets processed through software like AGMT3-D (Artifact GeoMorph Toolbox 3D) attempt to solve this by automatically aligning artifacts using the distribution of normal vectors and computing principal axes of inertia to establish a standardized coordinate system.60 Similarly, the StyroStone protocol relies on rigid scanning matrices, embedding small artifacts in radiolucent styrofoam for uniform Micro-CT capture.62  
When integrating external PLY, STL, and WRL files from OSF, Zenodo, and Sketchfab, the platform's ingestion pipeline must include an automated, orientation-agnostic preprocessing layer. Algorithms must utilize Procrustes superimposition and principal component analysis (PCA) to spatially align all incoming meshes along a common centroid and primary axis before feature extraction begins.1 If a Clovis point from the COADS dataset is ingested at a 45-degree skew relative to an Aurignacian bladelet, the resulting edge angle and elongation outputs will be catastrophically distorted. The classifier must learn to dynamically rotate objects based on their moment of inertia, orienting platforms proximally and terminations distally.65

### **Resolution Normalization and Format Parsing**

The raw data exists in a fragmented ecosystem of file types. While the platform requests PLY, OBJ, or STL, datasets like the Levantine Handaxes utilize WRL and VRML.11 The ingestion framework must automatically parse these disparate formats, strip extraneous vertex color or texture data (which holds no morphometric value and artificially inflates file size), and convert the geometry into a unified, lightweight polygon format suitable for tensor manipulation.27  
Furthermore, datasets scanned via Micro-CT possess vertex counts orders of magnitude denser than those scanned via photogrammetry or structured light.1 To prevent the classifier from inadvertently learning to identify the *scanning methodology* rather than the *artifact morphology*, all incoming meshes must be subjected to quadric edge decimation or similar re-meshing algorithms to normalize polygon counts across the entire training corpus.27

### **The Shift from Categorical Typologies to Continuous Morphospace**

The taxonomic labels accompanying these datasets are inherently subjective. The archaeological definition of a "Nubian Levallois Core" relies on specific cultural interpretations that do not seamlessly translate to machine logic.29 When the classifier is fed data spanning Acheulean handaxes 10, Protoaurignacian bladelets 7, and Holocene projectile points 68, strict categorical boundaries begin to collapse into a continuous multidimensional morphospace.  
Advanced research indicates that lithic typologies are rarely discrete entities, but rather unbroken continua of reduction intensity, raw material constraints, and functional adaptation.5 Therefore, the classifier should not be restricted to outputting rigid categorical labels (e.g., Class: Core, Blank: Flake). Instead, by leveraging the diverse datasets identified in this report, the machine learning architecture should be trained to output continuous geometric probabilities. For instance, rather than simply labeling a specimen from the COADS dataset as a "Biface," the algorithm should output a high-dimensional embedding that maps its exact volumetric symmetry, edge concavity, and scar density.24 This ensures that the platform functions as an objective morphometric tool, capable of identifying subtle techno-functional transitions across time, rather than acting as a digital proxy for subjective typological debates.26

The evolution of automated lithic analysis is no longer constrained by a lack of three-dimensional data. The open-access ecosystem—driven by platforms like Zenodo, OSF, and institutional dataverses—now hosts an abundance of high-fidelity, millimeter-scale meshes that capture the entire temporal and geographical breadth of hominin stone tool production. By augmenting the existing 2,016 Upper Palaeolithic meshes with the \~2,500 highly specific models identified in this report (pushing the total well past the 4,500 mark and approaching the 5,000+ target), a morphometric classifier will transition from a narrow regional tool into a universally applicable analytical engine. This diversified training corpus ensures the platform will learn the underlying physical and geometric realities of lithic fracture, empowering it to extract highly nuanced morphometric features, estimate volumetric loss, and objectively map the continuous, unbroken spectrum of prehistoric technological design.
